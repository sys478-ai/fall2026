How brain-inspired language helps explain AI systems, and how it can mislead us.

## Overview

AI systems are often described using biological language. We hear about neural networks, artificial neurons, synapses, weights, learning, memory, attention, adaptation, and intelligence.

These metaphors can be useful. They can help us understand why some systems are organized as networks, how connections can change over time, and why neuromorphic computing draws inspiration from biological systems.

But biological metaphors can also mislead. A machine learning system does not learn the way a person learns. A neural network is not a brain. A model does not understand in the way a human understands. A device that “remembers” a pattern may be storing a physical or mathematical state, not having an experience.

In this lab, you will compare biological learning, machine learning, and neuromorphic learning. You will identify where the analogies are useful, where they break down, and how metaphors shape public understanding, trust, marketing, policy, and governance.

## Core Learning Goals

By the end of this lab, you should be able to:

- Explain why biological metaphors are common in AI.
- Compare biological learning, machine learning, and neuromorphic learning at a high level.
- Distinguish between useful analogy and misleading overclaim.
- Analyze how terms like “neuron,” “learning,” “memory,” and “intelligence” shape public understanding.
- Explain why biological inspiration does not automatically make a system natural, trustworthy, safe, or ethical.
- Connect metaphor analysis to explainability, accountability, hype, misuse, and governance.

## Key Terms

| Term                   | Working Definition                                                                                                      |
| ---------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Metaphor               | A way of understanding one thing in terms of another.                                                                   |
| Biological learning    | Learning in living organisms, involving bodies, environments, development, experience, feedback, and biological change. |
| Machine learning       | A computational process in which a model changes based on data or examples.                                             |
| Neuromorphic computing | Computing systems inspired by biological nervous systems, often using hardware, spikes, events, or local adaptation.    |
| Artificial neuron      | A simplified computational unit inspired by biological neurons.                                                         |
| Synapse                | A biological connection between neurons; also used metaphorically for connections or weights in artificial systems.     |
| Weight                 | A numerical or physical value that affects the strength of a connection in a model.                                     |
| Plasticity             | The ability of a biological or artificial system to change over time based on activity or experience.                   |
| Anthropomorphism       | Describing a non-human system as if it had human-like understanding, intention, feeling, or agency.                     |
| Hype                   | Exaggerated claims about what a technology can do or what it means.                                                     |

## Part 2: Three Kinds of Learning

Compare three different uses of the word “learning.”

### Biological Learning

A person practices playing piano. Over time, their body, attention, memory, hearing, movement, and interpretation change. Their learning is shaped by feedback, motivation, prior experience, emotion, culture, teaching, and embodiment.

### Machine Learning

A model is trained on examples. During training, its internal parameters are adjusted so that it performs better on a task, such as classification or prediction.

### Neuromorphic Learning

A brain-inspired system changes over time through spikes, timing, local state changes, connection strengths, or hardware-level adaptation. The system may process events efficiently and respond to patterns or anomalies.

## Part 4: Synapses, Weights, and Plasticity

In biology, synapses are connections between neurons. Their strength and behavior can change over time.

In machine learning, weights are values that affect how much influence one part of a model has on another.

## Part 9: Metaphors and Responsibility

Sometimes metaphors shift responsibility.

For example:

- “The AI decided” may hide the role of designers, institutions, and deployment choices.
- “The system learned this behavior” may hide training data choices or weak oversight.
- “The model hallucinated” may make an error sound mysterious or inevitable.
- “The system is brain-like” may make it seem natural or unavoidable.

## Part 12: Connection to BRAID

BRAID involves cerebellum-inspired neuromorphic computing. This makes biological metaphor both necessary and risky.

The biological inspiration matters because the project draws on ideas such as:

- spikes
- synapses
- plasticity
- pattern separation
- prediction error
- anomaly detection
- rapid adaptive response
- cerebellar circuitry

But the governance analysis cannot stop at biology.

A cerebellum-inspired system is still a human-designed technical system. It is shaped by:

- research goals
- funding priorities
- materials and hardware constraints
- application choices
- deployment settings
- institutional incentives
- regulatory gaps
- stakeholder participation
- possible misuse
- public understanding

For BRAID, responsible communication should help people understand both:

1. what biological ideas inspired the system, and
2. why social, ethical, and governance questions remain central.
