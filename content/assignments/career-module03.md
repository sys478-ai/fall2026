## Overview

This module asks you to connect career ideas with financial realities. You will estimate cost of living, research salary ranges, and consider how this information affects your post-graduation plans.

It also introduces salary negotiation as an important part of career planning. Most students will begin below the median salary for a field and work upward over time, but starting salary still matters because it shapes later raises and long-term earnings. Some positions are non-negotiable, but many are not, and negotiation is often most effective when you first receive an offer.

### 4. Salary Negotiation Basics

Salary negotiation is easiest when it is grounded in solid information. Three kinds of information matter especially:

<!-- .compact -->

1. what you need to live
1. what the field typically pays
1. what strengths, skills, and accomplishments you bring to the position

Your budget helps you identify the salary range you could realistically accept. The low end of that range is your "walk away" number: the point where accepting the job would not make sense financially. The high end is your stretch goal. In many cases, it makes sense to think in terms of a range rather than one fixed number, because ranges leave more room for negotiation.

When employers hear a range, they often focus on the middle. If an application asks for an expected salary and allows only one number, you can enter the middle of your range.

Today, we are focusing on the first two parts of that process: budget and salary research. In the next module, we will build the third part by identifying the strengths, skills, and accomplishments you can use to support a salary conversation.
