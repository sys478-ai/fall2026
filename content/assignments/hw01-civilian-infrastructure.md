Scenario handout for the [BRAID Group Anticipatory Analysis](/fall2026/assignments/hw01).

## Overview

This page contains three possible civilian infrastructure scenarios involving a neuromorphic edge chip designed for anomaly detection.

Your group should choose **one** scenario for your deeper analysis.

For your chosen scenario, pay attention to:

- what the chip is monitoring
- whether the model appears to be fixed, adaptive, or hybrid
- what happens after an anomaly is flagged
- who is affected by the system's output

For any scenario, the model might be:

- **Fixed:** calibrated before deployment and not substantially changed unless manually updated.
- **Adaptive:** updated over time based on what the device encounters in use.
- **Hybrid:** allowed to adapt within preset limits or only after review.

Do not try to analyze the entire infrastructure domain. Choose one specific scenario.

## Scenario 1: Aging Water System Monitoring

A city installs neuromorphic edge chips in sensors attached to parts of its water infrastructure. The sensors monitor pressure, flow, vibration, acoustic signals, or other patterns that may indicate leaks, blockages, contamination risks, pump problems, or pipe failure.

The sensors may be placed underground, inside pump stations, or in other locations that are difficult to inspect regularly. Some parts of the system may have limited connectivity or may go long periods without direct maintenance.

The system looks for changes such as sudden pressure drops, unexpected flow changes, unusual pipe vibrations, acoustic patterns associated with leaks, or pump behavior that differs from normal operation. If the system treats a change as unusual, it may log the event, alert municipal workers, trigger a maintenance request, or mark a pipe segment or pump station for inspection.

## Scenario 2: Transit System Monitoring

A city transit agency installs neuromorphic edge chips in sensors on buses, trains, tracks, elevators, escalators, or station equipment. The sensors monitor vibration, temperature, timing, movement, acoustic signals, or other patterns that may indicate equipment problems or service disruptions.

The system operates across many neighborhoods and transit routes. Some equipment is old, some is newly installed, and usage patterns may vary by time of day, season, event schedules, weather, or service changes.

The system looks for changes such as unusual wheel or track vibration, overheating motors, irregular elevator movement, delayed door timing, abnormal braking patterns, or acoustic signals associated with worn parts. If the system treats a change as unusual, it may log the event, alert maintenance staff, slow or stop equipment, change inspection priorities, or affect service scheduling.

## Scenario 3: Public-Space Infrastructure Monitoring

A city installs neuromorphic edge chips in sensors connected to streetlights, public plazas, pedestrian corridors, or other shared public spaces. The sensors monitor movement, sound, crowding, vibration, heat, lighting, or other environmental patterns.

The system operates in spaces used by residents, commuters, visitors, vendors, unhoused people, delivery workers, emergency responders, and city staff.

The system looks for changes such as unexpected crowding, repeated movement near restricted infrastructure, sudden loud sounds, unusual heat patterns, vibration near public equipment, or activity at unusual times of day. If the system treats a change as unusual, it may log the event, alert city personnel, activate additional lighting or sensing, notify emergency responders, or trigger another city response depending on how the system is designed.
