## Overview

This page contains three possible environmental and agricultural monitoring scenarios involving a neuromorphic edge chip designed for anomaly detection.

Your group should choose **one** scenario for your deeper analysis.

For your chosen scenario, pay attention to:

- what the chip is monitoring
- whether the model appears to be fixed, adaptive, or hybrid
- what kinds of changes the system treats as unusual
- what happens after an anomaly is flagged
- who is affected by the system's output

For any scenario, the model might be:

- **Fixed:** calibrated before deployment and not substantially changed unless manually updated.
- **Adaptive:** updated over time based on what the device encounters in use.
- **Hybrid:** allowed to adapt within preset limits or only after review.

Do not try to analyze the entire environmental or agricultural domain. Choose one specific scenario.

## Scenario 1: Precision Agriculture Monitoring

A farm installs neuromorphic edge chips in field sensors that monitor soil moisture, temperature, humidity, plant stress, pest activity, or irrigation patterns.

The sensors may be spread across large fields and may operate in areas with limited connectivity or limited access to power. Conditions may vary across the field because of soil differences, shade, slope, irrigation patterns, weather, or crop type.

The system looks for changes such as sudden drops in soil moisture, unusual temperature patterns, signs of plant stress, unexpected pest activity, or irrigation behavior that differs from normal operation. If the system treats a change as unusual, it may log the event, alert a farm manager, trigger an irrigation adjustment, recommend inspection, or contribute to a pesticide or crop-management decision.

## Scenario 2: Watershed Monitoring

A local government, research team, or conservation organization installs neuromorphic edge chips in sensors placed along streams, rivers, wetlands, or reservoirs. The sensors monitor water temperature, flow, turbidity, chemical signals, acoustic patterns, or other signs of environmental change.

The sensors may operate in remote locations, flood-prone areas, or places where connectivity is unreliable. Conditions may change because of storms, drought, seasonal cycles, construction, runoff, or long-term climate shifts.

The system looks for changes such as sudden shifts in water flow, unusual temperature changes, increased turbidity, unexpected chemical readings, or acoustic patterns associated with blockages or erosion. If the system treats a change as unusual, it may log the event, alert environmental staff, mark a location for inspection, or contribute to decisions about water management or public warnings.

## Scenario 3: Wildlife Habitat Monitoring

A conservation organization, research team, land agency, or tribal land office installs neuromorphic edge chips in sensors placed in a wildlife habitat or protected ecosystem. The sensors monitor sound, movement, temperature, light, vibration, species activity, or other environmental patterns.

The sensors may operate in remote areas where battery life, weather exposure, and limited connectivity matter. The area may change over time because of seasonal migration, fire, drought, invasive species, habitat restoration, human activity, or climate change.

The system looks for changes such as unexpected movement patterns, shifts in animal calls, unusual activity at certain times of day, changes in temperature or light patterns, or signs of human or vehicle activity in sensitive areas. If the system treats a change as unusual, it may log the event, alert land managers or researchers, mark an area for review, or contribute to decisions about conservation, access, or intervention.
