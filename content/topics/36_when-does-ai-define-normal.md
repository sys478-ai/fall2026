## Topic / Focus

Anomaly detection systems identify what falls outside a baseline. But who defines the baseline, what counts as anomalous, and what happens to people and behaviors that get flagged as deviating from the norm?

This topic uses anomaly detection — including neuromorphic systems like BRAID — as a case study for examining how AI systems construct normality, encode risk, and create new categories of deviance. The technical choices involved in setting baselines and thresholds are not neutral: they carry assumptions about what is standard, what is suspicious, and whose behavior is the reference point.

## Guiding Questions

- How does a system determine what is normal?
- Who or what sets the baseline, and what gets flagged as anomalous?
- Who bears the cost when the threshold is wrong?
- How is normality constructed differently in different contexts — a workplace, a hospital, a school, a city?
- What makes a threshold contestable, and by whom?

## In This Class

In this meeting, we will:

- examine how anomaly detection systems define and enforce normality
- analyze how baselines encode assumptions about standard behavior
- connect threshold design to questions of risk, accountability, and contestability
- use BRAID as a concrete case where these questions have institutional stakes
