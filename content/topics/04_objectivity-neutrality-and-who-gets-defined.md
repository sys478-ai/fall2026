## Topic / Focus

This topic asks what happens when intelligence becomes something to measure. Once institutions try to assess intelligence through tests, categories, labels, scores, or behavioral signals, they must decide what counts, how it will be measured, and what kinds of differences matter.

Measurement can make intelligence seem objective, but the act of measuring always depends on prior choices. Someone has to define the category, choose the criteria, decide what evidence is relevant, and determine how results will be interpreted. Those steps are often treated as technical or scientific, even though they are also social and institutional.

In this class, we examine how categories such as intelligent, normal, gifted, delayed, adaptive, suspicious, or anomalous are not simply discovered in the world. They are constructed through systems of measurement. That construction matters because once categories are formalized, they can travel through schools, workplaces, medical settings, technical systems, and public policy.

By the end of this topic, you should be able to explain why the measurement of intelligence is never just descriptive and why categories can carry real consequences long before any AI model is trained.

## Guiding Questions

- What kinds of evidence are used to measure intelligence?
- How do tests and categories shape what becomes visible?
- What gets excluded when a messy human capacity is turned into a metric?
- How do categories travel from institutions into technical systems?
- When does measurement begin to look objective simply because it is standardized?

## In This Class

## Looking Ahead

After examining how intelligence becomes measurable, we turn more directly to neutrality and authority. The next topic asks who or what gets defined by supposedly objective systems and what happens when those definitions are treated as natural.

## Recognition Patterns

- Classification is not neutral.

This pattern matters here because measurement usually depends on categories that appear stable only after they have been built. Once a category is standardized, it can become harder to see the values, exclusions, and assumptions that made it possible in the first place.

## BRAID / ELSI Connection

BRAID makes this issue concrete through technical terms such as signal, noise, expected behavior, and anomaly. Before a system can detect an anomaly, someone must define what the normal pattern is supposed to be. That baseline is not just a technical fact. It is a measurement decision with ethical and governance implications.

This topic helps students see that the same questions apply across many domains: who sets the baseline, what evidence counts, who gets misread by the system, and what happens when measured categories are treated as neutral truths.
