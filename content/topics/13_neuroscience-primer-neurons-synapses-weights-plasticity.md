## Topic / Focus

This topic asks why predictive performance is so often treated as evidence of intelligence. When a system makes a useful forecast, completes a pattern, or anticipates what comes next, people often begin to describe it as smart, adaptive, or even understanding.

That move is powerful but risky. Prediction is a real technical capability, yet it is narrower than many public uses of the word intelligence. Students need to be able to see both sides at once: why prediction feels impressive, and why it does not settle bigger questions about understanding, judgment, or meaning.

By the end of this topic, you should be able to explain why prediction can look like intelligence and why that resemblance should be analyzed rather than taken for granted.

## Guiding Questions

- Why does successful prediction seem intelligent?
- What is the relationship between prediction and historical data?
- What can prediction do well that people often associate with intelligence?
- What does prediction leave out?
- Why does the appearance of intelligence matter for public trust and policy?

## Recognition Patterns

- Prediction imports the past.
- AI systems can encourage anthropomorphism, aliveness, or sentience claims.

## BRAID / ELSI Connection

Anomaly detection systems often seem intelligent because they anticipate, flag, or respond to unusual conditions. This topic helps students separate that predictive capability from broader claims about understanding, awareness, or agency.
