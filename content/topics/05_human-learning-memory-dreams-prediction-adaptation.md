## Topic / Focus

This topic focuses on the authority of supposedly objective definitions. Once systems of measurement are in place, their outcomes can begin to look neutral, natural, or inevitable. But objectivity claims often hide the earlier decisions that made those outcomes possible.

We ask who gets to define intelligence, competence, normality, risk, or error, and what happens when those definitions are treated as unbiased simply because they are formalized. In many institutional settings, definitions gain power not because they are universally true, but because they are measurable, repeatable, and backed by technical or bureaucratic authority.

This topic helps students distinguish between two different questions: whether a system is consistent within its own rules, and whether those rules should be accepted in the first place. That distinction becomes crucial later in the course, when we study models that may be statistically accurate within a narrow framework but still reinforce damaging categories or institutional priorities.

By the end of this topic, you should be able to explain why neutrality claims deserve scrutiny and why the power to define a category is itself a social and political issue.

## Guiding Questions

- What makes a category or measurement system appear objective?
- When do technical or institutional definitions gain authority?
- Who benefits when definitions are treated as neutral?
- Who is at risk of being misdefined, overdefined, or excluded?
- What is the difference between internal consistency and ethical legitimacy?

## Recognition Patterns

- Classification is not neutral.

This pattern matters here because neutrality is often the story categories tell about themselves after the difficult work of defining, sorting, and enforcing them has already happened. Looking closely at who or what gets defined helps make those hidden judgments visible again.

## BRAID / ELSI Connection

BRAID raises these questions whenever a system distinguishes expected from unexpected behavior, useful from irrelevant signal, or safe from risky conditions. Those technical distinctions can look neutral because they are formal and measurable, but they still depend on human definitions of what should count.

This topic prepares students to ask not only whether a future system works, but also whose definitions it carries forward, whose behavior it treats as standard, and who gets to challenge the categories embedded in its design.
