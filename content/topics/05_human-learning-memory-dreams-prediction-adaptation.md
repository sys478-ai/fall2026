## Topic / Focus

This topic asks students to slow down around the word learning before it gets attached to machines. Human learning is not one single process. It can be biological, developmental, social, cultural, embodied, tool-mediated, and shaped by care, repetition, environment, and institutions.

That matters for AI because machine learning borrows the language of learning while referring to a much narrower set of technical operations. Before students study those systems, they need a richer picture of what learning means in human life and why not all kinds of change, memory, or adaptation are equivalent.

By the end of this topic, you should be able to describe several dimensions of learning and explain why those distinctions matter when technical systems call themselves adaptive or intelligent.

## Guiding Questions

- How does learning happen in bodies, relationships, and environments?
- What role do tools, institutions, and culture play in learning?
- How is human learning different from simple repetition, memorization, or prediction?
- What gets lost when the term learning is narrowed to a technical process alone?
- Why does it matter to distinguish human learning from machine learning?

## In This Class

In this meeting, we will:

- examine how memory, repetition, feedback, and context shape learning
- prepare for later questions about intelligence and machine learning

## Recognition Patterns

- AI systems can encourage anthropomorphism, aliveness, or sentience claims.

This pattern matters here because public conversations often slide too quickly from machine output to human-centered language like learning, understanding, or thinking. A richer account of human learning helps students resist that shortcut.

## BRAID / ELSI Connection

BRAID draws on biological inspiration, which makes it especially important to distinguish between human learning, animal learning, neural plasticity, and technical adaptation. Those analogies can be illuminating, but they can also overstate what a system is actually doing.

This topic prepares students to ask later whether a neuromorphic or adaptive system is learning in a meaningful sense, or whether it is better understood as responding to signals within a narrower technical frame.
