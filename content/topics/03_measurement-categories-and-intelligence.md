## Topic / Focus

This topic asks how knowledge gets made rather than treating it as something that simply appears on its own. In both everyday life and technical systems, knowledge depends on choices about what counts as evidence, who is trusted to interpret it, which institutions certify it, and what kinds of uncertainty are tolerated.

That matters for AI because datasets, models, and outputs are often treated as if they deliver objective knowledge automatically. Before students can evaluate those claims, they need language for thinking about interpretation, expertise, classification, and the difference between raw observation and justified belief.

By the end of this topic, you should be able to explain why knowledge is produced through methods and institutions, why claims of objectivity deserve scrutiny, and how those questions prepare us to analyze later AI systems.

## Guiding Questions

- What counts as evidence?
- How do interpretation and authority shape what people accept as knowledge?
- Which institutions get to certify knowledge claims?
- When does a measurement or output begin to look more objective than it really is?
- How do these questions carry over into AI systems and data practices?

## Recognition Patterns

- Classification is not neutral.

This pattern matters here because knowledge practices sort, prioritize, and normalize certain kinds of evidence while ignoring others. What looks like a neutral description of reality often depends on earlier judgments about what should count in the first place.

## BRAID / ELSI Connection

BRAID draws on research claims, engineering choices, and public narratives about intelligence, sensing, and efficiency. This topic helps establish that none of those claims should be treated as self-validating just because they come wrapped in technical language or institutional authority.

It also prepares students to ask later whether a system is measuring something meaningful, borrowing a scientific metaphor loosely, or using technical outputs to make contested claims look more settled than they are.

**Psychology, Cognitive Science, Neuroscience, and the Learning Sciences**

**Speaker notes:** This class asks how different fields study intelligence. The point is not to master each field in depth. The point is to see that intelligence is not one settled thing. Different fields define it differently, study it differently, and treat different kinds of evidence as meaningful.

### Slide 2: Big Question

**How do different fields define and study intelligence?**

- What counts as intelligence?
- What counts as evidence?
- What gets emphasized?
- What gets left out?

**Speaker notes:** This is the central framing for today. Every field brings methods, assumptions, and priorities. Those choices do not just help us observe intelligence. They help shape what intelligence is taken to be.

### Slide 3: Why This Matters

**Why this matters for this course**

- AI inherits ideas about intelligence.
- Measurement is never neutral.
- Technical systems borrow scientific language.
- Public claims about AI often sound more settled than they are.

**Speaker notes:** Later in the course, we will talk about learning, prediction, anomaly detection, and neuromorphic systems. All of those discussions depend on earlier assumptions about what intelligence is. If we treat those assumptions as obvious, we miss the politics of measurement and definition.

### Slide 4: Psychology

**Psychology**

- Often studies individual differences.
- Often uses tests, scales, and comparisons.
- Asks how people vary in reasoning, memory, processing, or performance.

**Strength:** Creates structured comparisons.

**Risk:** Can narrow intelligence to what is testable.

**Speaker notes:** Psychology is one of the major homes of intelligence research, especially in its testing tradition. A key move here is to make intelligence measurable across people. That can be useful, but it can also make a specific kind of measurable performance seem like the whole story.

### Slide 5: Cognitive Science

**Cognitive Science**

- Often studies mental processes.
- Focuses on memory, attention, reasoning, language, and problem-solving.
- Often uses models of how minds process information.

**Strength:** Helps explain how thinking may work.

**Risk:** Can overemphasize abstract cognition.

**Speaker notes:** Cognitive science often asks what kinds of information-processing operations are happening. This approach is especially important because AI often borrows its language: memory, attention, learning, representation. But modeling cognition is not the same thing as capturing the full reality of intelligence.

### Slide 6: Neuroscience

**Neuroscience**

- Studies brains, neural systems, and biological mechanisms.
- Looks at neurons, circuits, plasticity, sensing, and adaptation.
- Asks what is happening biologically during cognition and learning.

**Strength:** Grounds claims in physical mechanisms.

**Risk:** Brain evidence can sound more authoritative than it really is.

**Speaker notes:** Neuroscience can tell us a lot about biological mechanisms, but it does not automatically settle the meaning of intelligence. This matters because brain-inspired AI and neuromorphic computing often gain credibility by borrowing from neuroscience, even when the comparison is partial or metaphorical.

### Slide 7: Learning Sciences

**Learning Sciences**

- Studies how people learn in context.
- Emphasizes environment, interaction, pedagogy, culture, and practice.
- Asks how learning happens across settings, relationships, and tools.

**Strength:** Keeps intelligence and learning social and situated.

**Risk:** Harder to reduce to one clean metric.

**Speaker notes:** The learning sciences are especially helpful if we want to resist the idea that intelligence is just an internal trait. This field reminds us that learning depends on context, support, community, design, and environment. That can complicate simple measurement, but it also makes the picture more realistic.

### Slide 8: Comparison

**What each field tends to emphasize**

| Field             | Tends to Emphasize     | Often Misses                  |
| ----------------- | ---------------------- | ----------------------------- |
| Psychology        | Measurable differences | Context and power             |
| Cognitive science | Mental processes       | Embodiment and institutions   |
| Neuroscience      | Biological mechanisms  | Social meaning and governance |
| Learning sciences | Context and practice   | Clean standardization         |

**Speaker notes:** This slide is deliberately simplified, but it gives students a usable comparison. No field is wrong. Each field highlights something important. The problem comes when one approach gets treated as complete and neutral.

### Slide 9: Why This Matters for AI

**From intelligence research to AI claims**

- AI borrows terms like learning, memory, attention, and intelligence.
- Those borrowed terms can clarify.
- They can also mislead.
- Scientific language can give technical systems extra authority.

**Speaker notes:** This is where the topic connects directly to the rest of the course. When companies or researchers describe systems as learning, adapting, attending, or thinking, they are drawing on these intellectual traditions. Sometimes that is useful. Sometimes it overstates what the system is actually doing.

### Slide 10: Transition

**Key takeaway**

- Intelligence is not one neutral category.
- Different fields study it differently.
- Methods shape definitions.
- Next question: what happens when intelligence becomes measurable, categorized, and standardized?

**Speaker notes:** Today's goal is not to reject all measurement or all science. It is to make visible that intelligence is always being framed through methods and institutions. That sets up the next class, where we look more directly at measurement, categories, and the consequences of defining intelligence in standardized ways.
