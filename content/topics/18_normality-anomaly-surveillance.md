## Topic / Focus

This topic brings the strands of Module 3 together by introducing neuromorphic computing more directly. Students examine spiking neural networks, event-driven signaling, timing, and architectures designed to process information differently from conventional von Neumann-style systems.

The goal is not just to admire novelty. It is to understand what technical problem neuromorphic systems are trying to solve, what kinds of efficiency or responsiveness they promise, and what new questions they raise about explanation, materiality, and governance.

By the end of this topic, you should be able to explain the basic idea of neuromorphic computing and why its design choices matter both technically and socially.

## Guiding Questions

- What makes a neuromorphic system different from a conventional one?
- Why do spikes, timing, and event-driven computation matter?
- What kinds of efficiency or responsiveness are neuromorphic systems supposed to offer?
- How do these architectures complicate explanation and oversight?
- Why are material design choices also governance choices?

## Recognition Patterns

- AI is material: it depends on energy, water, chips, labor, mining, and waste.
- Opacity shifts authority.

## BRAID / ELSI Connection

This is the clearest technical entry point into the BRAID case. It gives students the vocabulary to discuss spikes, timing, adaptive hardware, and event-driven anomaly detection without treating the system as magical or immaterial.
