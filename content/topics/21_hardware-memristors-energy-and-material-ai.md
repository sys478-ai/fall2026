## Topic / Focus

This topic turns to healthcare and biosensing, where AI is often framed as helpful, protective, and life-improving. Clinical decision systems, diagnostic assistance, wearable devices, wellness tracking, and biosensing technologies all promise earlier detection, better monitoring, and more personalized support.

That promise is ethically powerful, but it also creates difficult questions. Health data can easily become surveillance data. False positives and false negatives can carry real bodily consequences. Systems designed for care may also intensify monitoring, narrow complex conditions into simplified signals, or shift responsibility onto patients and clinicians in new ways.

By the end of this topic, you should be able to explain how AI in healthcare and biosensing can be both supportive and risky, and why vulnerability and accountability matter so much in this domain.

## Guiding Questions

- What kinds of good are healthcare AI systems supposed to produce?
- When does monitoring become care, and when does it become surveillance?
- Who bears the cost of false alarms or missed detections?
- How do wearables and biosensing systems change responsibility?
- What kinds of human judgment should remain central in healthcare settings?
