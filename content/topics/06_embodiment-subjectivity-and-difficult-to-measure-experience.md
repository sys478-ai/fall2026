## Topic / Focus

This topic closes Module 1 by asking what counts as intelligence after we have already examined ethics, knowledge, and learning. Rather than treating intelligence as a single obvious property, we compare several possible meanings: reasoning, problem-solving, adaptation, memory, social coordination, embodied skill, and forms of care or situated judgment.

This matters because AI claims often rely on a narrow picture of intelligence. If intelligence is defined mainly as prediction, optimization, classification, or performance on a benchmark, then some kinds of systems will look far more intelligent than they otherwise might. Other forms of intelligence, especially embodied, relational, or context-sensitive ones, may disappear from view.

By the end of this topic, you should be able to compare several conceptions of intelligence and explain why those definitions shape later AI design, evaluation, and public interpretation.

## Guiding Questions

- What different forms of intelligence do people recognize?
- Which forms of intelligence are rewarded, measured, or ignored?
- What happens when intelligence is equated with prediction or optimization?
- What kinds of intelligence are relational, embodied, or context-dependent?
- Why do definitions of intelligence matter for AI?

## Recognition Patterns

- AI systems can encourage anthropomorphism, aliveness, or sentience claims.

This pattern matters here because students need practice noticing when intelligent-seeming behavior is being mistaken for a richer form of mind, agency, or understanding than the system actually possesses.

## BRAID / ELSI Connection

BRAID is especially useful here because neuromorphic systems can sound brain-like, adaptive, or even lifelike in ways that exceed their actual technical scope. Students need a careful vocabulary for talking about intelligence before they encounter those claims in more technical detail.

This topic sets up the rest of the semester by asking what kind of intelligence AI systems are actually claiming, simulating, or borrowing language from.
