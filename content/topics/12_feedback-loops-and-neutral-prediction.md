## Topic / Focus

This topic asks what kinds of mistakes matter, to whom, and under what conditions. Once model outputs become decisions, errors no longer remain statistical abstractions. They become missed opportunities, false alarms, wrongful suspicion, wasted time, medical risk, or institutional neglect.

Different systems distribute those costs differently. Some contexts treat false positives as acceptable and false negatives as dangerous; others reverse the tradeoff. That means error is always technical, ethical, and political at the same time.

By the end of this topic, you should be able to explain why error costs are not evenly distributed and why accountability questions begin at the point where systems make consequential mistakes.

## Guiding Questions

- What is the difference between a false positive and a false negative?
- Which kind of error matters most in a given use case, and why?
- Who absorbs the cost when a system makes a mistake?
- How do thresholds redistribute harm and benefit?
- Why is accountability often hard to locate after automated error?

## Recognition Patterns

- Thresholds make uncertainty consequential.
- Automation changes accountability.

## BRAID / ELSI Connection

In BRAID-like systems, the cost of a missed anomaly may look very different from the cost of a false alarm. This topic helps students ask not only whether detection works, but what kinds of mistakes a system is built to tolerate and who pays when it gets them wrong.
