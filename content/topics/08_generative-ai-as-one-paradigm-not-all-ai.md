## Topic / Focus

This topic stays with data, but shifts from data creation to data inheritance. Once categories, baselines, and features are chosen, they carry assumptions about what matters, what counts as normal, and what kinds of variation the system is allowed to ignore.

This is where students begin to see that the data layer is never just a neutral input layer. Datasets reflect institutions, measurement practices, design goals, and histories. Features do not merely describe the world; they shape what the model will be able to learn from it.

By the end of this topic, you should be able to explain how values, exclusions, and institutional priorities get built into datasets and feature sets before model training begins.

## Guiding Questions

- What assumptions are built into a dataset?
- What makes a feature seem reasonable or useful?
- How do proxies smuggle in value judgments?
- What kinds of people, behaviors, or conditions are likely to be misrepresented or excluded?
- Why can a technically clean dataset still encode a messy social history?

## In This Class

In this meeting, we will:

- inspect how features and labels carry assumptions
- compare direct measurements to proxies
- identify exclusions and blind spots in datasets
- connect data design to later questions about learning, error, and governance

## Recognition Patterns

- Features are value choices.
- Categories are constructed choices.

## BRAID / ELSI Connection

In BRAID-like systems, signal encodings and anomaly baselines are not neutral engineering details. They are the point where judgments about relevance, normality, and actionable difference get built into the system.
