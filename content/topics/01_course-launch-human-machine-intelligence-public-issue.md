## Topic / Focus

This opening meeting frames the course by treating intelligence as a public question rather than only a technical one. We begin with familiar claims about “smart” machines, human intelligence, automation, and AI in everyday life, then ask what those claims leave out.

The goal is not to settle the definition of intelligence on day one. The goal is to begin noticing that intelligence is always described through institutions, values, measurements, metaphors, and infrastructures. When people say that a system is intelligent, efficient, adaptive, or autonomous, they are not only describing a technical mechanism. They are also making claims about what kinds of behavior matter, what kinds of judgment count, and what kinds of authority should be trusted.

This meeting also introduces the course’s larger rhythm: we will move back and forth between technical mechanisms, ethical recognition patterns, and governance questions. Throughout the semester, we will return to the idea that AI systems are never just code. They are built from people, hardware, data, labor, stories, institutions, and decisions about what should be optimized, measured, or acted upon.

By the end of this topic, you should be able to explain why AI needs to be studied as both a technical system and a public problem.

## Guiding Questions

- What do people usually mean when they call a system “intelligent”?
- What assumptions are built into everyday talk about AI?
- How do technical systems become public issues?
- What gets hidden when AI is described as if it were only software?
- Why does it matter to study AI through labor, hardware, infrastructure, institutions, and governance?

## Looking Ahead

This topic sets up the rest of Module 1. In the next meetings, we will ask what kinds of good AI is supposed to produce, what counts as intelligence, how different fields study it, and what kinds of human experience should place limits on technical ambition.

## Recognition Patterns

- AI systems are socio-technical systems.

This pattern matters at the start of the course because it keeps us from treating AI as a self-contained object. Instead of asking only whether a model is accurate or impressive, we ask what infrastructure, labor, design choices, institutions, and public consequences make the system possible.

## BRAID / ELSI Connection

The BRAID case study in this course focuses on an emerging cerebellum-inspired neuromorphic circuit for anomaly detection in edge computing. Even before students learn the hardware details, this first meeting establishes an important principle: BRAID is not only a chip or a lab prototype. It is also a social and institutional project involving researchers, funders, use cases, public claims, governance questions, and possible downstream impacts.

That means BRAID should be approached from the beginning as a socio-technical system. As the semester continues, we will keep asking who designs such systems, who benefits from them, who may be harmed by them, what kinds of oversight are possible, and what assumptions are built into their technical goals.

## Notes

Keep Day 1 light. This meeting should introduce the course, establish AI as a socio-technical and public issue, and give students a shared vocabulary for the semester without turning into a full moral philosophy session.

If ethical framing comes up here, keep it simple: AI is not neutral because it is built by people and institutions for particular purposes. Save the more explicit framework discussion for Day 2.
