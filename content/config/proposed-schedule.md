# Proposed Course Schedule

## Module 1. AI-Mediated World: Purposes, Values, and Questions

- **Week 1 | Tue, Aug 18 | Where Is AI Mediating the World Around Us?**
  Map AI systems students already encounter across everyday life, institutions, infrastructure, and culture. Briefly introduce the five guiding questions as the recurring analytical frame for the semester.
  *Rationale: Keep as opening session. Also use it to introduce the 5 guiding questions explicitly so students have the analytical lens from day one.*

- **Week 1 | Thu, Aug 20 | Why Is AI a Public and Ethical Issue?**
  Frame AI as a technical project that redistributes visibility, authority, risk, and responsibility in public life.
  *Rationale: Keep as proposed. Public/ethical framing motivates the course without requiring students to apply evaluative tools they have not yet learned.*

- **Week 2 | Tue, Aug 25 | What Is AI Supposed to Help Us Do?**
  Examine how claims about safety, care, efficiency, creativity, and improvement shape what AI is designed and justified to do.
  *Rationale: Keep as proposed. Anchors the course in human purposes before mechanism.*

- **Week 2 | Thu, Aug 27 | What Tools Will Help Us Take a Position?**
  Preview the five course guiding questions as analytical tools. Introduce ethical frameworks as vocabulary to develop all semester rather than conclusions to reach now.
  *Rationale: Changed from "What Ethical Lenses Help Us Evaluate AI?" Students cannot meaningfully apply ethical frameworks before they understand how AI works. Use this session to preview the 5 guiding questions explicitly and introduce ethical frameworks as tools they will develop and apply throughout the course. The full applied session moves to Week 7.*

- **Week 3 | Tue, Sep 1 | What Is Knowledge, and How Is It Made?**
  Examine how evidence, interpretation, institutions, and authority shape what counts as knowledge before and after AI enters the scene.
  *Rationale: Keep as proposed.*

- **Week 3 | Thu, Sep 3 | What Is Learning, and How Does It Happen?**
  Compare biological, developmental, social, tool-mediated, and computational learning to avoid reducing learning to model training.
  *Rationale: Keep as proposed.*

- **Week 4 | Tue, Sep 8 | What Counts as Intelligence?**
  Compare human, animal, social, embodied, and machine intelligence while asking what each definition emphasizes or leaves out.
  *Rationale: Moved into Module 1. This is a definitional question in the same register as "What Is Knowledge?" and "What Is Learning?" — all three ask students to examine a concept they think they already understand. Module 2 then opens cleanly with a technical question.*

## Module 2. Technical and Social Mechanisms of Machine Learning

- **Week 4 | Thu, Sep 10 | How Does the World Become Data?**
  Introduce sensing, measurement, categories, baselines, proxies, and features as technical and social acts of translation.
  *Rationale: Keep as proposed.*

- **Week 5 | Tue, Sep 15 | What Gets Built Into the Data?**
  Use datasets, proxies, and feature design to examine how assumptions, exclusions, histories, and institutional priorities enter AI systems.
  *Rationale: Keep as proposed.*

- **Week 5 | Thu, Sep 17 | How Do Machines Learn from Data?**
  Introduce models, training, inference, and evaluation as the basic pipeline through which data becomes computational judgment.
  *Rationale: Keep as proposed.*

- **Week 6 | Tue, Sep 22 | What Kind of Learning Is This?**
  Distinguish supervised, unsupervised, reinforcement, and related learning paradigms by asking what feedback the system receives.
  *Rationale: Keep as proposed.*

- **Week 6 | Thu, Sep 24 | How Do Scores Become Decisions?**
  Trace how model outputs become scores, rankings, thresholds, alerts, recommendations, and institutional actions.
  *Rationale: Keep as proposed.*

- **Week 7 | Tue, Sep 29 | Who Bears the Cost of Error?**
  Examine how false positives, false negatives, and uncertainty distribute burdens differently across people and contexts.
  *Rationale: Keep as proposed.*

- **Week 7 | Thu, Oct 1 | What Ethical Lenses Help Us Evaluate AI?**
  Apply utilitarianism, care ethics, deontology, and the common good to concrete AI mechanisms students now understand: error, thresholds, training data, and scores.
  *Rationale: Moved here from Week 2. Students now have enough knowledge of mechanism, error, and thresholds to apply ethical frameworks to real cases. This is when the frameworks grip something concrete. The prediction-as-intelligence topic from this slot folds into Week 8 Thu.*

- **Week 8 | Thu, Oct 8 | Are Predictions Ever Neutral?** *(Fall Break Tue — no class)*
  Analyze how prediction systems reuse history, reinforce patterns, and appear to make systems smarter than they are. This session absorbs the prediction-as-apparent-intelligence topic from Week 7.
  *Rationale: Keep as proposed. Finishes Module 2 after the break. Module 3 begins Week 9.*

## Module 3. Brain-Inspired, Neuromorphic, and Hardware-Level AI

- **Week 9 | Tue, Oct 13 | When Does AI Define Normal?**
  Use anomaly detection to examine how baselines and thresholds construct normality, deviance, risk, and contestability.
  *Rationale: Keep as proposed. Good bridge from prediction and error into neuromorphic anomaly detection.*

- **Week 9 | Thu, Oct 15 | Why Are Neural Networks Hard to Trust?**
  Connect neural-network opacity to explanation, over-reliance, contestability, documentation, and accountability.
  *Rationale: Keep as proposed.*

- **Week 10 | Tue, Oct 20 | What Can Neuroscience Tell Us?**
  Introduce basic neuroscience concepts while clarifying what brain metaphors can and cannot explain about AI systems.
  *Rationale: Keep as proposed.*

- **Week 10 | Thu, Oct 22 | How Does Neuromorphic Computing Work?**
  Introduce spiking, event-driven computation, timing, edge learning, and hardware-level AI as a different model of machine intelligence.
  *Rationale: Keep as proposed.*

## Module 4. AI in Institutions: Domains, Power, and Solutionism

- **Week 11 | Tue, Oct 27 | How Is AI Changing Work and Employment?**
  Examine hiring, monitoring, productivity scoring, and algorithmic management as systems that classify workers and redistribute workplace power.
  *Rationale: Changed. Move BRAID case study to Week 13 Thu after students have seen domain cases. Start Module 4 domain sequence here with work and employment — the most familiar institutional context for most students.*

- **Week 11 | Thu, Oct 29 | How Is AI Reshaping News, Politics, and Public Attention?**
  Use recommendation, generation, targeting, and ranking systems to examine visibility, persuasion, attention, and public influence.
  *Rationale: Domain sequence shifts up to fill space left by BRAID moving: work → media → healthcare → schools.*

- **Week 12 | Tue, Nov 3 | How Is AI Changing Healthcare and Biosensing?**
  Use clinical decision support, wearables, and biosensing to examine risk classification, care, monitoring, vulnerability, and consent.
  *Rationale: Domain sequence continues shifting up.*

- **Week 12 | Thu, Nov 5 | How Is AI Entering Schools and Other Social Institutions?**
  Use tutoring, proctoring, AI detection, monitoring, and administrative triage to examine authority, suspicion, support, and student sorting.
  *Rationale: Schools last in the domain sequence because students can reason from their own institutional experience.*

- **Week 13 | Tue, Nov 10 | How Do AI Systems Redistribute Power and Control?**
  Examine platforms, chips, cloud vendors, standards, repair pathways, and infrastructure as sites where AI power concentrates or becomes contestable.
  *Rationale: Keep in this position. After four domain cases, students can synthesize across contexts and prepare for the pivot into governance.*

---

## Module 5. Governance, Alternatives, and Emerging Futures

- **Week 13 | Thu, Nov 12 | BRAID Case Study: How Can We Govern an Emerging AI Before Its Uses Are Settled?**
  Apply anticipatory governance to neuromorphic anomaly detection, drawing on domain cases to ground the analysis in real institutional contexts students have now encountered.
  *Rationale: Moved here from Week 11 Tue. Students have now seen four domain cases and a power/control synthesis. They can do more grounded anticipatory governance because they understand how AI actually lands in institutions. BRAID opens the governance unit rather than interrupting the domain sequence.*

- **Week 14 | Thu, Nov 19 | When Does AI Become Technological Solutionism?** *(Senior Symposium Tue — no class)*
  Use domain cases to ask when AI reframes political, institutional, or social problems as technical optimization problems.
  *Rationale: Keep as proposed. Solutionism is a strong pivot from domain analysis to governance.*

- **Week 15 | Tue, Nov 24 | Who Is Affected?**
  Map stakeholders, benefits, burdens, missing publics, and governance questions around an AI system or emerging technology.
  *Rationale: Keep as proposed.*

- **Week 16 | Tue, Dec 1 | What Alternatives Are Still Possible?** *(Thanksgiving Thu — no class)*
  Consider redesign, repair, slower deployment, democratic governance, non-AI interventions, and the option to refuse use.
  *Rationale: Keep as proposed.*

- **Week 16 | Thu, Dec 3 | How Should We Govern Emerging AI?**
  Use NIST, the EU AI Act, OECD, and IEEE to sharpen questions about risk, oversight, documentation, rights, and accountability.
  *Rationale: Keep as proposed. Formal frameworks as synthesis rather than early introduction.*

## Module 6. Final Public Explainers and ELSI Presentations

- **Week 17 | Final exam period | What Should Others Understand?**
  Present a public-facing technical explainer and ELSI analysis that helps others reason about an AI system's mechanisms, stakes, and governance.
  *Rationale: Keep as proposed.*
