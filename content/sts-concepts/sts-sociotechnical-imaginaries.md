## What This Means

Technologies are never just tools for solving problems. They embody visions — collectively held ideas about what problems are worth solving, what the future should look like, and what kinds of people or institutions should be in charge of shaping it. Sheila Jasanoff and Sang-Hyun Kim call these sociotechnical imaginaries: shared frameworks, often implicit, that connect technical systems to pictures of desirable social orders.

Imaginaries are not just rhetoric or marketing. They shape research agendas by defining which problems seem important. They shape funding priorities by determining which applications seem promising. They shape governance frameworks by establishing which outcomes need to be prevented and which can be assumed to be beneficial. A technology built within a particular imaginary — say, one that imagines anomaly detection as a neutral security tool — will have different design choices, different deployment contexts, and different governance frameworks than one built within an imaginary that treats detection as an exercise of social control requiring democratic accountability.

Imaginaries are also distributed unevenly. The imaginary held by the researchers and engineers who build a system is often not the same as the imaginary held by the communities the system affects. This mismatch is not incidental — it is a structural feature of who is at the table when technologies are designed and whose visions get built in.

## Questions To Ask

- What vision of society does this technology assume or reinforce?
- Whose problems does it prioritize solving, and whose problems does it treat as fixed background conditions?
- What kinds of people or behaviors does the system treat as problems to be managed?
- What alternative futures does this imaginary foreclose or make harder to imagine?
- How does the imaginary differ between those who build the system and those most affected by it?
- What would it mean to contest the imaginary rather than just critique the system?

## Why This Matters

Sociotechnical imaginaries matter because they shape what questions get asked before a technology is built — which means they determine a great deal about what kinds of critique are possible after the technology is deployed. If the imaginary behind a system is never made explicit, it is very difficult to challenge the assumptions built into it.

This card is a theoretical foundation for the course capstone. The capstone asks you to reason about a technology before it is widely deployed — while choices are still open and futures are still contestable. Doing that well requires making the imaginaries behind the technology visible: what vision of the future is this system building toward, and is that a vision worth building?

## What Happened

In October 2017, Waterfront Toronto — a tripartite government agency overseeing redevelopment of Toronto's eastern waterfront — selected Sidewalk Labs, a subsidiary of Alphabet (Google's parent company), to co-develop Quayside, a twelve-acre underutilized industrial site on Lake Ontario. The project was described as an opportunity to build a new kind of urban neighborhood from scratch, incorporating digital infrastructure, sensors, and data-driven management systems from the ground up.

In June 2019, Sidewalk Labs published its Master Innovation and Development Plan (MIDP), a 1,500-page document describing the project's vision and proposed implementation. The MIDP described a neighborhood in which sensor networks would track pedestrian and vehicle movement, dynamic street configurations would respond to data in real time, environmental systems would be managed algorithmically, and an "urban data trust" would govern how data collected throughout the neighborhood was stored and used. The document proposed that Sidewalk Labs would take a leading role in designing and operating these systems, with revenues generated in part from the data collected and from intellectual property developed through the project.

Public opposition emerged early and grew through the MIDP process. Bianca Wylie, a civic technologist and open government advocate, became the most prominent critic, publishing a series of essays arguing that the project represented a transfer of public governance functions to a private corporation without democratic accountability. The campaign she helped lead, #BlockSidewalk, framed its objections in terms of who should control the infrastructure of public space and who should profit from data generated by residents' presence in a neighborhood. Privacy concerns were part of the objection, but Wylie consistently argued that the deeper problem was the imaginary itself: that the project treated cities as optimization problems to be solved through data collection rather than as democratic communities with their own governance structures.

On May 7, 2020, Sidewalk Labs announced the cancellation of the project, citing economic uncertainty generated by the COVID-19 pandemic. The MIDP, however, remained publicly available, and many of the proposals it contained — including the urban data trust concept and dynamic street configuration systems — were subsequently pursued in other contexts by the company's spin-offs and by other smart city ventures.

**Source:** Sidewalk Labs, *Master Innovation and Development Plan* (June 2019). Bianca Wylie, "Sidewalk Toronto: A Brazen and Ongoing Corporate Hijack of Democratic Process," Medium (2019). Sidewalk Labs cancellation announcement, May 7, 2020.

## How This Connects

The Sidewalk Toronto case illustrates sociotechnical imaginaries at an unusual level of visibility. Most technologies embed their social visions implicitly — in design choices, in what gets measured and what doesn't, in who the imagined user is. The MIDP was unusually explicit: it described in 1,500 pages what a neighborhood organized around data collection and algorithmic management would look like, what institutions would govern it, and what economic arrangements would sustain it. The imaginary was cities as information systems — places where movement, behavior, and infrastructure could be continuously measured, optimized, and monetized.

That imaginary shaped what counted as a solution and what didn't. Problems that could be addressed with sensors and algorithms — traffic management, energy use, street configuration — appeared prominently in the MIDP. Problems that required democratic governance, community ownership, or limits on data collection were treated as constraints to work around or as questions to be resolved later. Bianca Wylie's opposition was not simply about privacy — it was a direct contest of the imaginary. She was arguing that the vision of the city the MIDP was building toward was not a vision that reflected the interests or choices of the community that would live in it, and that no legitimate democratic process had authorized the imaginary at all. The cancellation of the project did not resolve that contest; it just ended one particular deployment of it.
