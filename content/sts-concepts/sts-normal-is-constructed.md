## What To Notice

No anomaly exists without a baseline. Before a system can flag something as unusual, risky, or suspicious, it must first define what counts as ordinary.

That baseline is not automatic. It is built from historical data, technical assumptions, institutional priorities, and choices about what to include, exclude, measure, and label. When you see the word “anomaly,” ask: anomalous compared to what, and according to whose expectations?

Pay attention to cases where difference is treated as evidence of danger or failure. A system may identify behavior as unusual simply because it falls outside its chosen baseline, even when that variation is harmless or legitimate.

## Questions To Ask

- How was normality defined in this system?
- Who or what supplied the baseline?
- When does anomaly detection become a tool of surveillance or social control?
- What kinds of legitimate variation might the system misread?
- What happens after something is flagged as anomalous?
- Is the system identifying risk, or simply identifying difference?
- What assumptions are built into the data used to establish the baseline?

## Why This Matters

AI systems can describe what is statistically common, but they cannot determine on their own what should be considered acceptable, healthy, fair, or desirable. When a baseline is treated as objective, historical patterns and institutional assumptions can begin to look like natural facts.

This concept is central to the BRAID case study because anomaly detection depends on decisions about what counts as normal and what should happen when something falls outside that range. Students can use this concept to examine how baselines are built, whose behavior they represent, and when deviation becomes a reason for scrutiny or intervention.
