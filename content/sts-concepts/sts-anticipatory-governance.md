## What This Means

Governance of emerging technologies typically happens in one of two modes: before a technology is widespread, when there is little evidence of harm but many choices are still open; or after a technology is widespread, when harms may be visible but the system is so embedded that challenging it is extremely difficult. Most governance happens in the second mode — which is to say, too late.

Anticipatory governance, developed by David Guston and colleagues, is a framework for doing the first kind of governance seriously. It argues that waiting for evidence of harm before asking governance questions is not neutral — it is a political choice that advantages those who benefit from deployment and disadvantages those who will bear risks. The argument is not that the future is fully predictable, but that foreseeable consequences can be reasoned about now, using available evidence and analogical reasoning, while democratic choices remain open and institutional alternatives have not yet been closed off.

Erik Fisher's related concept of midstream modulation makes the case for intervening during development — not just before or after. The researchers and engineers building a system make consequential choices every day about what to optimize, what to measure, and what to test. Engaging with those choices during development, rather than after deployment, is when intervention has the most leverage. Anticipatory governance is not the same as precaution — it does not require proving that a technology is dangerous before asking governance questions. It requires only taking governance questions seriously while choices are still open.

## Questions To Ask

- What choices are still open in this system's development — about design, deployment, governance, oversight?
- What consequences are foreseeable now, even without complete evidence?
- What analogical cases — similar systems, similar deployment contexts — suggest about likely trajectories?
- Who is at the table when these choices are made, and who is not?
- What would it mean to slow down, redirect, or refuse this technology at this stage?
- What governance questions should be asked before scale arrives?

## Why This Matters

This card describes both a scholarly framework and what students are being asked to practice. The course capstone is an exercise in anticipatory governance: reasoning carefully about a technology before it is widely deployed, using available evidence and analogical thinking, while choices are still open.

The BRAID neuromorphic chip is a direct case. It is an emerging technology, not yet widely deployed, with foreseeable consequences that can be reasoned about now. The capstone asks you to do that reasoning — to bring governance questions to a system at the stage where they can still matter, not after the system is embedded and alternatives have been foreclosed.

## Example A: The Asilomar Conference (1975)

### What Happened

In 1973, Stanford biochemist Paul Berg successfully combined DNA from two different organisms — a technique that quickly became understood as having broad applications but also unknown risks, including the possibility of accidentally creating novel pathogens. By 1974, a group of leading molecular biologists, including Berg, David Baltimore, and Maxine Singer, had become sufficiently concerned about the risks of recombinant DNA (rDNA) research that they took an unusual step: they publicly called for a voluntary moratorium on certain classes of rDNA experiments until the scientific community could assess the risks.

The moratorium held for roughly eight months. In February 1975, approximately 150 scientists, lawyers, and journalists from the United States and sixteen other countries convened at the Asilomar Conference Grounds in Pacific Grove, California, for a four-day meeting. The conference was organized primarily by Berg. Attendees included leading figures in molecular biology, along with legal experts who could speak to liability and regulatory questions. Press were admitted on the condition that reporting would be embargoed until the conference concluded.

On the final day of the conference, February 27, 1975, participants voted to end the moratorium and allow rDNA research to resume — but with new biosafety guidelines that classified experiments by risk level and specified physical and biological containment requirements for each. The conference created the NIH Recombinant DNA Advisory Committee, which met the same day. The NIH Guidelines for Research Involving Recombinant DNA Molecules were formally published in 1976. Over the following two decades, millions of rDNA experiments were conducted without documented public health incidents. The research enabled medical advances including recombinant insulin, clotting factor for hemophilia, and hepatitis B vaccines.

**Source:** Paul Berg et al., "Potential Biohazards of Recombinant DNA Molecules," *Science* 185 (July 26, 1974): 303 [the moratorium letter]. Asilomar Conference proceedings published in *Science* 188 (June 6, 1975). Historical account: Embryo Project Encyclopedia, "Asilomar Conference (1975)," Arizona State University.

### How This Connects

Asilomar is the canonical case of anticipatory governance in science and technology studies, and it is the model Guston and others explicitly draw on when arguing for applying similar principles to AI. The scientists did not wait for a documented disaster — they interrupted research in progress because they could foresee categories of risk, even if the specific probabilities were unknown. The choices made at Asilomar — what research to permit, at what containment levels, with what oversight mechanisms — were made while those choices were still genuinely open. The moratorium was only possible because it happened before the research was commercially embedded and before institutions with financial interests in continuing had organized to resist it. That timing is the entire point.

## Example B: The EU AI Act

### What Happened

In April 2021, the European Commission published a proposed regulation on artificial intelligence — a framework that would become the first comprehensive AI law by a major jurisdiction. The proposal had been in development since 2018, well before many of the AI systems it addressed were widely deployed in Europe. The drafting process involved multiyear public consultations, expert advisory groups, and extensive parliamentary debate. The European Parliament adopted the final text in March 2024, and the EU AI Act entered into force on August 1, 2024.

The Act established a tiered risk classification system. AI systems posing "unacceptable risk" — including real-time remote biometric identification of individuals in public spaces for law enforcement purposes, and untargeted scraping of facial images from the internet to build recognition databases — were prohibited outright. These prohibitions became effective in February 2025. A second tier of "high-risk" AI systems, including biometric categorization, credit scoring, and systems used in hiring and education, faced requirements for transparency, human oversight, accuracy testing, and registration in a public database. Full compliance obligations for high-risk systems are scheduled to apply from August 2026.

The debate over which systems counted as "high-risk" and which practices were prohibited was itself a site of anticipatory governance. Member state negotiators, civil society organizations, industry representatives, and Parliament debated questions like: Should general-purpose AI models be regulated separately? Should emotion recognition in workplaces be permitted? Should predictive policing tools be classified as high-risk? The negotiations over Article 5 (prohibited practices) and Annex III (high-risk systems) were, in effect, arguments about what futures the regulation should foreclose — conducted before those futures had fully arrived.

**Source:** Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 of the European Parliament and of the Council (the EU AI Act), entered into force August 1, 2024. European Parliament legislative timeline: europarl.europa.eu. Current text and article-level commentary at artificialintelligenceact.eu.

### How This Connects

The EU AI Act is an instance of anticipatory governance working — imperfectly, contested, with significant debates about scope and enforcement, but working. The prohibitions on real-time remote biometric identification and facial scraping were enacted before those practices were widely deployed in EU member states. The "high-risk" classification debate was a debate about which governance questions to ask before deployment rather than after. Guston's argument for anticipatory governance is precisely that regulatory frameworks should be built before technologies are embedded — because once they are embedded, the interests that depend on them have already organized, and the practical options for restriction have narrowed.

What the EU AI Act also illustrates is that anticipatory governance is not frictionless. The negotiations took years, involved significant industry lobbying, and produced a framework with implementation dates that extend years past passage. Not all of the hard governance questions were resolved. But those questions were asked — formally, publicly, with legislative force — while deployment was still limited. That is the structure Guston identifies as anticipatory governance, however imperfect the outcome.

## Example C: Facial Recognition in Law Enforcement

### What Happened

Facial recognition technology was adopted by US law enforcement agencies throughout the 2010s without a national regulatory framework governing its use, accuracy requirements, or due process protections. By 2020, the Government Accountability Office estimated that roughly half of adult Americans had their images in facial recognition databases accessible to law enforcement. The technology was integrated into investigative workflows at the federal level — including by the FBI, Immigration and Customs Enforcement, and the Department of State — and at hundreds of state and local agencies.

In December 2019, the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) published the first comprehensive demographic analysis of facial recognition algorithms. The NIST Face Recognition Vendor Test (FRVT) Part 3: Demographics evaluated 189 algorithms from 99 developers and found that the majority were between 10 and 100 times more likely to produce a false positive match for Black and East Asian faces compared with white faces, depending on the algorithm and test condition. Most algorithms were substantially less likely to correctly identify a Black woman than any other demographic group.

Several wrongful arrests attributable to facial recognition misidentification were documented in the years surrounding the report. Robert Williams, a Black man in Michigan, was arrested in January 2020 after a facial recognition system incorrectly matched him to surveillance footage of a shoplifting suspect; he spent thirty hours in jail before the error was identified. Nijeer Parks, a Black man in New Jersey, was arrested in January 2019 on charges later dropped, in part because facial recognition evidence was based on a false match. Michael Oliver, a Black man in Michigan, was similarly misidentified and arrested in 2019.

San Francisco became the first US city to ban municipal use of facial recognition technology in May 2019 — before the NIST FRVT findings were published. Over twenty other US cities and counties followed with similar ordinances. No federal legislation governing law enforcement use of facial recognition had passed as of mid-2026.

**Source:** NIST, *Face Recognition Vendor Test (FRVT) Part 3: Demographic Effects* (NISTIR 8280), December 2019. Kashmir Hill, "Wrongfully Accused by an Algorithm," *New York Times*, June 24, 2020 (Robert Williams case). Government Accountability Office, *Facial Recognition Technology: Federal Law Enforcement Agencies Should Better Assess Privacy and Other Risks* (GAO-21-518), June 2021.

### How This Connects

The facial recognition case shows what happens when anticipatory governance is not practiced. The technology was adopted by hundreds of agencies before accuracy disparities were formally documented, before governance frameworks were established, and before affected communities had formal mechanisms to contest its use. The NIST report in December 2019 established what STS researchers call the "evidence base" for governance — but by then, the technology was already embedded in operational law enforcement workflows at scale.

This is the asymmetry Guston identifies as the structural problem: those who benefit from rapid deployment have incentives to move quickly, while those who will bear the risks have no formal mechanism to require governance before deployment. The cost of waiting for evidence of harm is paid entirely by the people the technology is used against. By the time moratoriums and bans began passing, the question was no longer whether to adopt facial recognition but whether to limit or roll back a technology already in use by the federal government and dozens of state agencies. The moment for upstream governance had passed.
