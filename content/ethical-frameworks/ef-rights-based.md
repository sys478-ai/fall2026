## The Core Idea

Rights-based ethics holds that certain protections — privacy, due process, equal treatment, freedom from discrimination — are non-negotiable floors that cannot be bargained away even when doing so would produce aggregate benefit. Rights set limits on what can be justified by good outcomes alone.

This framework is especially important in AI governance debates, where rights-based arguments often cannot be resolved through better data or more careful cost-benefit analysis.

## The Diagnostic Question

**Does this system violate a protection that people are entitled to regardless of the outcomes it produces?**

## Questions To Ask

- Does this system affect rights to privacy, due process, equal treatment, or freedom from discrimination?
- Are those rights being traded off against efficiency or aggregate benefit?
- Who is able to assert those rights, and what practical process exists for doing so?
- Are affected people aware that the system may threaten or limit their rights?
- What legal or institutional frameworks exist to protect these rights, and are they being respected?

## Tensions and Limits

Rights frameworks require deciding which rights exist and how to resolve conflicts between them — questions that are genuinely contested. They can also be slow to respond when new technologies threaten rights in ways existing law did not anticipate. But they provide something other frameworks don't: a floor that holds even when the aggregate math looks favorable.

## Key Thinkers

**John Locke** (1632–1704) provided the foundational account of natural rights — to life, liberty, and property — that cannot be legitimately overridden by governments. His _Two Treatises of Government_ (1689) established rights as pre-political and inalienable, a framework that shaped liberal political thought and the language of human rights declarations.

**Eleanor Roosevelt** (1884–1962) chaired the committee that drafted the _Universal Declaration of Human Rights_ (1948) and played a leading role in building support for it, translating rights philosophy into international law and helping establish the framework that still shapes global human rights discourse.

**Ronald Dworkin** (1931–2013) developed one of the most influential contemporary rights theories in _Taking Rights Seriously_ (1977), arguing that rights function as "trumps" over aggregate welfare calculations — a direct challenge to utilitarian reasoning in law and policy.

**Martha Nussbaum** (1947–) developed the capabilities approach — a closely related framework that focuses not only on formal rights, but on what people are actually able to do and be. Her _Creating Capabilities_ (2011) is especially useful for evaluating systems that formally respect rights while undermining the conditions for their exercise.
