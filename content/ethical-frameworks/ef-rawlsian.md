## The Core Idea

John Rawls proposed that a fair system is one that rational people would choose if they were behind a "veil of ignorance" — not knowing their own position in society, their race, class, gender, or any other characteristic that might advantage or disadvantage them. If you didn't know whether you'd be the person helped or harmed by a system, would you still accept its design?

Applied to AI, this frame is a powerful test for threshold decisions, error distributions, and design choices that benefit some at the expense of others.

## The Diagnostic Question

**If you didn't know which side of this system's decisions you would land on, would you still accept how it was designed?**

## Questions To Ask

- Who benefits from this system's current design, and who bears its costs?
- Would the designers accept this system's error rates if they were subject to them?
- If the people most harmed by this system had designed it, what would be different?
- Does the system's design reflect the perspective of a particular group presented as universal?
- What would change about this system if its designers didn't know their own position?

## Tensions and Limits

The veil of ignorance is a thought experiment, not a procedure — real design processes involve people with known positions and interests. But it is a useful diagnostic: when a system's benefits and burdens are very unevenly distributed, asking whether the designers would accept that distribution if positions were reversed often reveals assumptions that were never made explicit.

## Key Thinkers

**John Rawls** (1921–2002) is the central figure. His _A Theory of Justice_ (1971) introduced the veil of ignorance and the difference principle — that inequalities are only just if they benefit the least advantaged members of society. It remains the most influential work in political philosophy of the twentieth century. His later _Political Liberalism_ (1993) refined the theory for pluralist societies.

**T.M. Scanlon** (1940–) developed a related but distinct contractualist framework in _What We Owe to Each Other_ (1998), arguing that an act is wrong if it violates principles that no one could reasonably reject. His approach shifts from hypothetical choice to reasonable rejection, making it more tractable for specific cases.

**Norman Daniels** (1942–) extended Rawlsian theory to healthcare and other institutions in _Just Health_ (2008), showing how the framework applies to the design of systems that affect life prospects — a direct application to AI systems operating in healthcare, education, and social services.

**Iris Marion Young** (1949–2006) offered an influential critique of Rawlsian distributive frameworks in _Justice and the Politics of Difference_ (1990), arguing that justice requires attending to structural oppression and cultural recognition, not only the distribution of goods. Her work is a useful counterpoint to purely distributional thinking.
