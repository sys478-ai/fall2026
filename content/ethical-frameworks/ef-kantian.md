## The Core Idea

Kantian ethics holds that people have inherent dignity and must never be treated merely as instruments for someone else's goals. Unlike utilitarian reasoning, Kantian ethics sets constraints that cannot be overridden by good outcomes: some things are wrong regardless of the aggregate benefit they produce.

Applied to AI systems, this frame asks: does this system use people — their data, their labor, their bodies, their risk — as raw material for goals they had no part in setting?

## The Diagnostic Question

**Is anyone in this system being treated as a means to an end rather than as a person with dignity and standing?**

## Questions To Ask

- Whose data, labor, or risk is being used to build or operate this system?
- Did those people have meaningful say in how they are used?
- Are the people bearing the most risk also the ones who would benefit?
- Would the system's designers accept being treated the same way?
- Is the system's efficiency or accuracy being achieved at the cost of someone's dignity or autonomy?

## Tensions and Limits

Kantian ethics can be demanding to apply in practice — almost any system uses people in some way. The more useful question is whether people are being treated *merely* as means, with no regard for their own ends. This frame is especially powerful for cases involving data extraction, invisible labor, and vulnerable populations used as test cases.

## Key Thinkers

**Immanuel Kant** (1724–1804) is the originator of the framework. His _Groundwork for the Metaphysics of Morals_ (1785) introduces the categorical imperative — the principle that one should act only according to rules one could will to be universal laws, and that rational persons must never be treated merely as means. These two formulations are the most frequently applied in ethical analysis of AI.

**W.D. Ross** (1877–1971) modified Kantian deontology in _The Right and the Good_ (1930), replacing absolute duties with "prima facie" duties — obligations that hold unless overridden by stronger competing obligations. This more flexible version is often more tractable in real-world cases where duties conflict.

**Christine Korsgaard** (1952–) is the leading contemporary Kantian moral philosopher, defending and extending Kant's framework in _The Sources of Normativity_ (1996) and _Creating the Kingdom of Ends_ (1996). Her work engages directly with challenges to Kantian ethics from consequentialism and constructivism.

**Onora O'Neill** (1941–) has applied Kantian ethics to questions of trust, information, and institutions, arguing that autonomy requires more than the absence of coercion — it requires the conditions under which rational agency is possible. Her work is directly relevant to informed consent and transparency in algorithmic systems.
