## The Core Idea

Libertarian ethics holds that individual freedom and voluntary consent are the primary moral values. People have the right to make their own choices, enter into voluntary agreements, and be free from coercion by governments or other actors. Applied to AI, this frame asks: did people freely choose to participate in this system, and is anyone being coerced?

This is the dominant ethical framework in much of tech culture, underlying arguments for minimal regulation, market competition as the primary accountability mechanism, and consent as the primary test of legitimacy.

## The Diagnostic Question

**Did people freely and voluntarily choose to participate in this system, and is anyone being coerced?**

## Questions To Ask

- Did people genuinely choose to use this system, or were they subject to it without meaningful alternatives?
- Is participation truly voluntary, or are there significant costs to opting out?
- Does this system involve government coercion, or private actors operating in a market?
- Are there competitive alternatives that give people real choices?
- Where the state is involved, is its role justified by preventing harm to others?

## Tensions and Limits

Libertarian reasoning is powerful for identifying genuine coercion, but tends to treat formal consent as sufficient even when real choice is limited by power, information asymmetry, or lack of alternatives. "Consent was obtained but wasn't meaningful" is a direct challenge to this frame from within its own logic. Libertarian arguments also struggle with systems where harms fall on third parties who never consented — bystanders caught by surveillance systems, communities affected by automated decisions they had no part in.

Understanding libertarian reasoning matters because it is genuinely influential in tech policy debates. Being able to engage with it — including identifying where it applies and where it breaks down — is more useful than dismissing it.

## Key Thinkers

**John Locke** (1632–1704) established the foundational libertarian premise: individuals have natural rights to life, liberty, and property that governments exist to protect, not override. His account of property rights and the limits of legitimate government shaped the liberal tradition from which both libertarianism and rights-based ethics draw.

**John Stuart Mill** (1806–1873) articulated the harm principle in _On Liberty_ (1859) — that the only legitimate basis for restricting individual freedom is preventing harm to others. This principle underlies most libertarian arguments against paternalistic regulation of technology.

**Robert Nozick** (1938–2002) is the canonical libertarian political philosopher. His _Anarchy, State, and Utopia_ (1974) argued that any redistribution of property beyond protecting individual rights violates those rights — a direct challenge to Rawlsian egalitarianism. His framework underlies arguments against mandatory data-sharing, platform regulation, and algorithmic auditing requirements.

**Friedrich Hayek** (1899–1992) contributed the epistemic argument for markets over planning in _The Constitution of Liberty_ (1960) and _The Road to Serfdom_ (1944): decentralized price signals aggregate more information than any central authority can, making market outcomes more efficient and more fair than designed alternatives. This argument is frequently deployed against algorithmic governance proposals.
