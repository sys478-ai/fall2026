## The Core Idea

Care ethics holds that moral reasoning should begin not with abstract principles or aggregate outcomes but with relationships — the particular bonds of dependency, trust, and responsibility between specific people. Associated with Carol Gilligan and Nel Noddings, it arose partly as a critique of ethics frameworks that treat persons as interchangeable rational agents. Care ethics insists that context, vulnerability, and relationship are not noise to be abstracted away; they are where moral life actually happens.

Applied to AI and automated systems, care ethics asks whether a system attends to the specific situation of the person it affects — or whether it processes them as an instance of a category. It is especially useful when systems intervene in relationships that were previously mediated by human judgment, discretion, and responsiveness: healthcare, social services, education, child welfare.

## The Diagnostic Question

**Does this system support or erode the relationships of care and trust that the people it affects depend on?**

## Questions To Ask

- Who bears vulnerability in this system, and whose needs is the system designed to be responsive to?
- Does automating this decision replace a relationship that carried moral weight — where someone was responsible for attending to a particular person?
- Does the system allow for the kind of contextual, individualized judgment that care sometimes requires, or does it enforce uniform treatment?
- Are the people most dependent on this system also the most powerless to contest its outputs?
- When the system makes an error, who absorbs the consequences — and do they have a relationship with anyone who is accountable?
- Does the system support accountable relationships of care, or merely simulate attentiveness while leaving no person responsible for responding to someone’s needs?

## Tensions and Limits

Care ethics can be accused of privileging existing relationships over fairness to strangers — of being too parochial. It also resists formalization: care is hard to encode, and attempts to do so often produce systems that simulate responsiveness without providing it. The framework is most useful as a corrective to the blind spots of more rule-based approaches — a reminder that particular people in particular situations are not well-served by being treated as generic cases.

## Key Thinkers

**Carol Gilligan** (1936–) launched care ethics as a field in _In a Different Voice_ (1982), arguing that dominant theories of moral development — particularly Lawrence Kohlberg's — were built on male-centered conceptions of justice and failed to account for an ethics grounded in relationships and responsiveness. Her work reframed what had been seen as a developmental deficit as an alternative moral voice.

**Nel Noddings** (1929–2022) developed care ethics as a systematic ethical theory in _Caring: A Feminine Approach to Ethics and Moral Education_ (1984), grounding it in the natural caring found in close relationships and asking how that caring could be extended and sustained.

**Virginia Held** (1929–) extended care ethics beyond personal relationships into political and social theory in _The Ethics of Care_ (2006), arguing that care should replace contract and market as the dominant organizing metaphor for social life.

**Joan Tronto** (1952–) brought care ethics explicitly into political theory in _Moral Boundaries_ (1993), analyzing how care work is systematically devalued and showing that who does care work — and who is exempted from it — is a question of power, not just personal virtue.
