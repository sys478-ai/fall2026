This module asks what happens when AI systems move from technical possibility into major domains of social life. Rather than organizing the material mainly by mechanism, the module turns to case studies: work and employment, news and politics, healthcare and biosensing, schools and other institutions, and the redistribution of power and control.

The goal is to help students see that AI does not arrive in the world as a neutral tool. It enters already-structured institutions with uneven power, competing incentives, and histories of inequality. In each domain, students ask not only what the system does, but who benefits, who is monitored, who loses discretion, who gains control, and what kinds of political or institutional questions are being reframed as technical ones.

1. `How Is AI Changing Work and Employment?`
2. `How Is AI Reshaping News, Politics, and Public Attention?`
3. `How Is AI Changing Healthcare and Biosensing?`
4. `How Is AI Entering Schools and Other Social Institutions?`
5. `How Do AI Systems Redistribute Power and Control?`

For BRAID, this is the main translational module. Students connect neuromorphic anomaly detection to concrete domains and ask how systems built for detection, efficiency, or adaptation may reshape labor, oversight, institutional authority, and public narratives about what kinds of problems technology should solve.
