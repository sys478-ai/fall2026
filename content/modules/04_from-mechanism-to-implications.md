This module asks students to move back and forth between technical mechanisms and their downstream implications. Anomaly detection, sensing, edge AI, hardware architectures, and environmental efficiency claims are treated as parts of larger socio-technical systems.

Students examine how "normal" is constructed, how sensors translate the world into data, and how local or on-device AI can change privacy, oversight, repair, and accountability. The module also considers how hardware vendors, standards, materials, and lifecycle costs shape who has power over emerging AI systems.

For BRAID, this is the main translational module. Students connect neuromorphic anomaly detection to questions about surveillance, false alarms, auditability, patching, energy use, environmental tradeoffs, and public explanation.
