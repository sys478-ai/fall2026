This module explains how messy parts of the world become data for machine learning systems, and how those systems turn data into models, scores, predictions, and decisions. Students begin with sensing, measurement, categories, baselines, and feature design, then move into model training, learning paradigms, scoring, thresholds, prediction, and feedback loops.

The module also asks who bears the costs of technical choices that appear neutral: which proxies carry historical bias, which error types fall on which communities, and how feedback loops can reinforce the patterns they were meant to correct. The module closes by returning to the ethical frameworks introduced in Module 1 — now equipped with enough technical vocabulary to apply them to concrete mechanisms.

Fall 2026 dates: `Thu, Sep 10 - Thu, Oct 8`

Meeting sequence:

1. `How Does the World Become Data?`
2. `What Gets Built Into the Data?`
3. `How Do Machines Learn from Data?`
4. `What Kind of Learning Is This?`
5. `How Do Scores Become Decisions?`
6. `Who Bears the Cost of Error?`
7. `What Ethical Lenses Help Us Evaluate AI?`
8. `Are Predictions Ever Neutral?`

For BRAID, this module prepares students to see that sensing, encoding, baselines, and thresholding are governance issues as well as technical ones. A hardware-level anomaly detector still depends on choices about what sensors capture, what counts as a relevant signal, what counts as normal behavior, and how mistakes should be handled.
