## What To Notice

AI systems are built by people with particular positions, resources, and relationships to power. When the people who will bear the most direct consequences of a system are not involved in designing it, their needs, contexts, and concerns tend to go unrepresented.

This isn't only about fairness in process — it produces worse systems. Edge cases that would be obvious to affected communities get missed. Assumptions get baked in that don't hold in practice. The system optimizes for what its designers could see, not for what matters to the people it acts on.

Look for: systems deployed in communities the developers have no direct relationship with; design processes where affected groups were consulted only after key decisions were made; systems where the people who bear the most risk also had the least access to shaping how risk is handled.

## Questions To Ask

- Who was in the room when this system was designed?
- Who was not in the room, and what might they have raised?
- At what stage were affected communities consulted — before key decisions or after?
- Who bears the most risk if this system fails or causes harm?
- What mechanisms exist for affected people to contest, correct, or refuse the system?

## Why This Matters

Participatory design and community input are not just procedural niceties — they are epistemic resources. People with direct experience of a problem know things that researchers and engineers typically don't. Excluding them doesn't make a system more objective. It makes it less accurate about the contexts that matter most.

This card pairs with "Concentrated harm is hidden by aggregate benefit": when affected communities have no voice in design, harms that fall disproportionately on them are less likely to be anticipated, measured, or reported.
