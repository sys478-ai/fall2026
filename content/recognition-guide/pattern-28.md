## What To Notice

Early deployment of AI systems is inherently experimental. Systems fail in ways that were not anticipated, cause harms that were not predicted, and require adjustment that only real-world use can reveal. Someone bears the risk of that learning process.

When pilot programs are consistently located in prisons, public housing, welfare systems, immigrant communities, or other contexts where people have limited ability to refuse or exit, those populations bear the experimental risk without having agreed to it — and often without being told. Meanwhile, if the system is later adopted more broadly, the benefits may be distributed more widely than the harms were.

Look for: pilot programs in institutions that serve people with little choice about participation; framing of surveillance or assessment tools as services or benefits; deployment in public or carceral settings before private or voluntary ones.

## Questions To Ask

- Who is bearing the risk of this system's early deployment?
- Did those people choose to participate, or were they subject to it?
- Would this system be piloted on a population with more power to refuse?
- What recourse do affected people have if the system causes harm?
- Who will benefit if this system is eventually scaled — and is that the same population bearing current risk?

## Why This Matters

This pattern reflects a broader dynamic in which the costs of technological development are socialized downward — toward people with less power, fewer resources, and less ability to contest decisions. Virginia Eubanks's work in *Automating Inequality* documents how AI systems are routinely deployed first on poor and marginalized communities, under the argument that these settings provide useful data and represent high-need environments, while the populations themselves have no meaningful say.
