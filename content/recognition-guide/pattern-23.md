## What To Notice

Measurement isn't passive observation. When an institution decides what to count, how to count it, and where to draw the boundary of what qualifies, it shapes what enters the official record — and what disappears from it.

This matters because official records are what policy responds to. If a death is not counted, it doesn't appear in statistics, doesn't trigger investigation, and doesn't generate accountability. The underlying event still happened. But officially, it didn't.

Watch for moments when measurement boundaries shift: reporting windows expand or contract, definitions are revised, categories are added or removed, data collection is discontinued. These are rarely neutral technical adjustments. They determine what kinds of harms become visible and which remain invisible.

## Questions To Ask

- What changed in how this was measured — and when?
- What becomes visible or invisible as a result of this change?
- Who made the decision to change how this is measured, and under what pressure?
- What would have been counted under the old definition that no longer is?
- Whose interests are served by the current measurement boundary?

## Why This Matters

This pattern is one of the most important in the field guide because it shows that data is not a neutral record of reality. It is a product of choices — about what to track, for how long, and under what conditions. When those choices change, reality as officially recorded changes with them, even when nothing on the ground did.

This connects directly to how public accountability works: oversight bodies, journalists, advocates, and affected communities all depend on official records. Shrinking or shifting what gets measured can quietly eliminate the evidence base for accountability.
