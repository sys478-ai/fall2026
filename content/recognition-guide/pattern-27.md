## What To Notice

The automation narrative presents AI systems as replacing human labor. In practice, most AI systems depend on vast amounts of human work that is structured to be invisible: workers who label training data, review and filter content, handle edge cases the algorithm can't, and clean outputs before they reach end users.

This labor is often performed by workers in precarious conditions — low wages, contract or gig arrangements, geographic concentration in lower-income regions, exposure to harmful content without adequate support. The system's performance depends on their work. Their conditions and contributions are rarely mentioned when the system is described or marketed.

Look for: claims that a system is "fully automated"; systems that improved dramatically after a particular data collection or annotation campaign; content platforms that present moderation as algorithmic when significant human review is involved.

## Questions To Ask

- What human labor is required to make this system function?
- Who performs that labor, under what conditions, and at what pay?
- How is that labor described — or not described — when the system is presented?
- What would the system look like without this work?
- Who benefits from keeping this labor invisible?

## Why This Matters

Invisibilizing labor has material consequences: it suppresses wages, limits worker power, and allows institutions to avoid accountability for working conditions. It also distorts public understanding of AI systems — making them appear more autonomous, more technical, and less social than they actually are. Mary Gray and Siddharth Suri's term "ghost work" names this phenomenon directly.

**Key reading:** Mary Gray and Siddharth Suri, *Ghost Work: How to Stop Silicon Valley from Building a New Global Underclass*, 2019.
