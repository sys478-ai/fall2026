## What To Notice

Consent is often treated as a binary: either it was obtained or it wasn't. But the quality of consent matters as much as its presence. Consent that was given without understanding, without a real alternative, in a moment of vulnerability, or in conditions of significant power imbalance may be formally valid but practically hollow.

Consider: a terms-of-service agreement that is 30 pages long; a wellness program that is technically optional but tied to insurance premiums; a form signed in a bureaucratic process where refusal means losing access to services; data collection disclosed in dense legal language that most people won't read. Consent was obtained. But it wasn't meaningful.

Look for: consent obtained through fine print; opt-out structures rather than opt-in; situations where refusing has significant costs; conditions where people had no real alternative.

## Questions To Ask

- Was this consent informed — did people actually understand what they were agreeing to?
- Was there a real option to refuse, or would refusal carry significant costs?
- How much power did the person have relative to the institution asking for consent?
- Was the consent given in a situation of vulnerability, urgency, or dependency?
- What were people told the data would be used for — and how does that match what it was actually used for?

## Why This Matters

The legal fiction of consent can be used to transfer risk and responsibility from institutions to individuals. When consent is used as a shield against accountability rather than as a genuine mechanism for respecting autonomy, it undermines both trust and protection. This card often pairs with "Data collected for one purpose gets used for another" — the consent that was technically obtained usually covered the original use, not the secondary one.
