## What To Notice

Averages can mask distributions. When a system's overall performance looks good — accuracy rates, efficiency gains, cost savings — that number combines the experiences of many different groups. If harms fall disproportionately on a subset of people, the aggregate figure can still look positive even as those people are significantly worse off.

This is especially important when the groups bearing the most harm are also less represented in the training data, less likely to be surveyed, or less likely to have the resources to contest errors.

Watch for: evaluation metrics reported only in aggregate; system rollouts where benefits and costs don't fall on the same population; efficiency gains that accrue to institutions while errors fall on individuals.

## Questions To Ask

- Are performance metrics broken down by group — or reported only in aggregate?
- Who benefits from this system, and who bears the risk of its errors?
- Do the benefits and harms fall on the same people?
- How was success defined, and who defined it?
- What would this number look like if you disaggregated it by race, income, geography, or other relevant factors?

## Why This Matters

A system that is accurate 95% of the time can still be systematically wrong about a specific group. If error rates aren't examined by subgroup, disparate harm can go undetected — and unaddressed — while the system looks like it's working. This card helps you push past summary statistics to ask who is inside the average, and who is being averaged away.
