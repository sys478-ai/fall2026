## What To Notice

Innovation stories often highlight novelty, convenience, speed, personalization, or efficiency. Those benefits may be real, but they can also hide what is being taken, transferred, or made newly available for profit or control.

This pattern asks you to look for extraction underneath the language of progress.

## Questions To Ask

- What is being captured, scraped, measured, mined, or monetized?
- Whose data, labor, attention, creativity, environment, or public infrastructure makes the system valuable?
- Who receives the benefits, and who bears the costs?
- What forms of consent, compensation, refusal, repair, or public accountability are available?
- Does the system create value by making existing social resources easier to appropriate?

## Examples

### Training data and creative work

Generative AI systems may be described as breakthroughs in creativity or productivity, but their value can depend on large collections of writing, images, code, music, and other cultural work. When those materials are gathered without meaningful consent, attribution, or compensation, innovation can also become a way to extract creative labor and public culture.

### Workplace monitoring and productivity tools

Software that promises smarter management or efficiency can turn workers' time, movement, communication, and behavior into data. The system may be presented as optimization, but it can also shift power toward employers or vendors by making labor more visible, measurable, and controllable.

### Smart infrastructure and public data

Sensors, platforms, and edge AI systems can be sold as modern infrastructure. But they may also turn public space, community behavior, or municipal services into proprietary data streams. Public value can be converted into private assets unless governance, procurement, and accountability are designed carefully.

## Why This Matters

This pattern helps you pause before accepting innovation language at face value. It does not assume every new system is exploitative. Instead, it asks what kinds of value the system depends on, who controls that value, and whether people or communities have meaningful power over what is being taken from them.
