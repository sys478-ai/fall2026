## What Happened

Allegheny County in Pennsylvania uses a predictive risk model to assist child protective services (CPS) intake workers when they receive a hotline call about a child. After a call comes in, the model generates a risk score from 1 to 20 based on a range of administrative data, including public benefits history, involvement with the justice system, and child welfare history.

The county set a threshold at 14. If a case scores above that threshold, a "high risk" flag is shown to the intake worker. Below it, the flag is not shown. The intake worker still makes the final decision about whether to open an investigation, but the score and flag are visible during that decision.

The threshold at 14 is a policy choice made by the county. It could be set higher or lower, and each position produces a different distribution of false positives (families flagged who were not at elevated risk) and false negatives (cases not flagged that turned out to involve harm). These errors fall differently: the costs of false negatives are concentrated and visible (harm to a child); the costs of false positives are diffuse and harder to track (disruption to families who were wrongly scrutinized, including the surveillance itself as a harm).

**Source:** Virginia Eubanks, [_Automating Inequality: How High-Tech Tools Profile, Police, and Punish the Poor_](https://us.macmillan.com/books/9781250074317/automatinginequality), St. Martin's Press, 2018. See also: Rhema Vaithianathan et al., "Developing predictive risk models to support child maltreatment hotline screening decisions," _Lucile Packard Foundation_, 2017.
