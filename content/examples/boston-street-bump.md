## What Happened

Boston tested a smartphone app called Street Bump that used phone accelerometers to detect potholes as residents drove. The appeal was straightforward: passive, real-time road condition data without sending inspectors everywhere.

But the resulting dataset depended on who owned a smartphone, who installed the app, where they drove, how their phone was mounted, and which neighborhoods were already connected to city services. Wealthier, more connected neighborhoods generated more data. Low-income neighborhoods and areas with lower smartphone adoption were underrepresented.

The map that emerged was not simply a map of potholes. It was a map of participation, wealth, and municipal attention — shaped by who could and did contribute data.

**Source:** Kate Crawford, ["The Hidden Biases in Big Data,"](https://www.hbr.org/2013/04/the-hidden-biases-in-big-data) _Harvard Business Review_, 2013.
