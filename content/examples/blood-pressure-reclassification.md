## What Happened

In November 2017, the American College of Cardiology (ACC) and the American Heart Association (AHA) jointly released new blood pressure guidelines that lowered the threshold for a hypertension diagnosis from 140/90 mmHg to 130/80 mmHg.

The change was based on evidence from clinical trials suggesting elevated cardiovascular risk at lower blood pressure levels than previously recognized. The guideline writers judged that the evidence supported treating more people to prevent more heart attacks and strokes.

The numerical consequence was immediate: approximately 31 million additional Americans became hypertensive by definition. Their blood pressure had not changed overnight. What changed was where the boundary of the "hypertensive" category was drawn.

The reclassification had real downstream effects. People newly labeled hypertensive became candidates for medication, monitoring, lifestyle interventions, and clinical follow-up. Insurers and employers classify and price risk using diagnostic categories. Individuals experience a change in self-understanding and health identity.

This example is not an argument that the guideline change was wrong — the evidence it was based on was real. It illustrates that diagnostic thresholds are decisions about where to draw lines that produce categories, not discoveries of natural boundaries in biology.
