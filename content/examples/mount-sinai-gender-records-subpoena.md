## What Happened

In June 2026, the Mount Sinai Health System disclosed that it had received a grand jury subpoena demanding information about adolescent patients who had received gender-related care. The subpoena was issued by the United States District Court for the Northern District of Texas.

Mount Sinai was the second Manhattan hospital system to publicly confirm a federal investigation of this kind. NYU Langone had disclosed a similar subpoena the previous month, demanding information "sufficient to identify each patient" under 18 who received gender-related treatments since 2020.

The Trump administration had sought to end gender-transition treatments for adolescents, and the Justice Department had issued more than 20 subpoenas to hospitals nationwide over the prior year. Many hospitals had already shut down their pediatric transgender health programs in response to the investigations and threats to federal funding.

Mount Sinai's spokeswoman said the hospital intended to provide only "de-identified information, with all patient-identifying details removed" if ultimately required to comply. But parents of affected patients were skeptical that anonymization would protect their children. One mother said she received a call from a Mount Sinai social worker informing her that anonymized records from her 17-year-old son's file would be shared with the federal government. She said her family was "in panic mode" and felt "betrayed" by the institution.

New York law required the hospital to notify affected patients in the event that records were produced.

In some earlier cases, hospitals successfully challenged subpoenas, with judges finding they had been issued for an improper purpose. But those rulings tended to favor hospitals in states where the treatments remained legal. Because the Mount Sinai subpoena came from a Texas grand jury, any legal challenge would have to be litigated in Texas — a significant barrier.
