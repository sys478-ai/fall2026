## What Happened

Credit scoring systems such as FICO produce a numerical output — a score between 300 and 850 — intended to represent creditworthiness. But the score is an output of a model. What the score means in practice — whether someone can get a mortgage, at what rate, or with what down payment requirement — depends on thresholds that lenders, regulators, and secondary market institutions set separately.

A score of 620 has long been treated as a rough threshold for conventional mortgage eligibility. Below it, borrowers are often classified as subprime and face higher rates, stricter terms, or denial. But this threshold is not derived from the model. It is a policy choice that reflects lenders' judgments about acceptable risk, regulatory requirements, and secondary market purchase criteria.

The errors on either side of this line fall asymmetrically. A lender approving a loan to someone who defaults incurs a financial loss. An applicant denied credit who would have repaid bears that denial — and its downstream effects on housing access, wealth accumulation, and financial stability — in ways that are diffuse and harder to track.

**Source:** For a general account of FICO scoring and mortgage thresholds, see reporting in consumer finance journalism and Federal Reserve research on credit access. For the asymmetry of credit error costs, see Natalie Campisi, "What Is A Good Credit Score?" _Forbes_, updated 2024.
