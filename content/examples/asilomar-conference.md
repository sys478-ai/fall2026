## What Happened

In 1973, Stanford biochemist Paul Berg successfully combined DNA from two different organisms — a technique that quickly became understood as having broad applications but also unknown risks, including the possibility of accidentally creating novel pathogens. By 1974, a group of leading molecular biologists, including Berg, David Baltimore, and Maxine Singer, had become sufficiently concerned about the risks of recombinant DNA (rDNA) research that they took an unusual step: they publicly called for a voluntary moratorium on certain classes of rDNA experiments until the scientific community could assess the risks.

The moratorium held for roughly eight months. In February 1975, approximately 150 scientists, lawyers, and journalists from the United States and sixteen other countries convened at the Asilomar Conference Grounds in Pacific Grove, California, for a four-day meeting. The conference was organized primarily by Berg. Attendees included leading figures in molecular biology, along with legal experts who could speak to liability and regulatory questions. Press were admitted on the condition that reporting would be embargoed until the conference concluded.

On the final day of the conference, February 27, 1975, participants voted to end the moratorium and allow rDNA research to resume — but with new biosafety guidelines that classified experiments by risk level and specified physical and biological containment requirements for each. The conference created the NIH Recombinant DNA Advisory Committee, which met the same day. The NIH Guidelines for Research Involving Recombinant DNA Molecules were formally published in 1976. Over the following two decades, millions of rDNA experiments were conducted without documented public health incidents. The research enabled medical advances including recombinant insulin, clotting factor for hemophilia, and hepatitis B vaccines.
