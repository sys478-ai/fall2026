## What Happened

Pulse oximeters are clipped to a finger to measure blood oxygen saturation (SpO2) using light absorption. They are widely used in hospitals and increasingly in consumer health devices.

Research published during the COVID-19 pandemic confirmed a problem that had been documented in smaller studies for decades: pulse oximeters are less accurate in patients with darker skin tones, tending to overestimate blood oxygen levels. A study published in the _New England Journal of Medicine_ in 2020 found that Black patients were nearly three times as likely as white patients to have occult hypoxia — dangerously low oxygen levels that the device failed to detect.

The problem originates in calibration. Pulse oximeters calculate SpO2 by passing light at two wavelengths through tissue. The calibration curves that relate the light absorption ratios to oxygen levels were developed primarily using data from lighter-skinned subjects. Darker skin melanin affects light absorption in ways the standard calibration doesn't account for.

During the COVID-19 pandemic, pulse oximetry was central to triage decisions, hospital admission criteria, and monitoring of patients recovering at home. Falsely reassuring readings in darker-skinned patients may have contributed to delayed treatment decisions.

**Source:** Michael W. Sjoding et al., ["Racial Bias in Pulse Oximetry Measurement,"](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMc2029240) _New England Journal of Medicine_, 2020. For historical documentation, see also Valerie Bhatt et al. in _CHEST_, 2021.
