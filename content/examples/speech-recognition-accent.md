## What Happened

A 2020 study published in the _Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences_ tested five major commercial automatic speech recognition (ASR) systems from Amazon, Apple, Google, IBM, and Microsoft against a corpus of audio recordings from 42 white speakers and 73 Black speakers.

The study found that all five systems had substantially higher word error rates for Black speakers than for white speakers. The average word error rate for Black speakers was 35%, compared to 19% for white speakers — meaning roughly one in three words was transcribed incorrectly for Black speakers on average.

The researchers attributed the disparity primarily to dialect differences: speakers of African American English (AAE) use grammatical structures and pronunciations that diverge from the Standard American English patterns that dominated the training corpora. The systems had been built around a particular way of speaking, and performance on that way of speaking defined what counted as accuracy.

The real-world stakes of ASR accuracy include transcription of job interviews, courtroom proceedings, medical dictation, and call center interactions — contexts where error rates translate into consequential disadvantages for the people whose speech is less accurately transcribed.

**Source:** Allison Koenecke, Andrew Nam, Emily Lake, Joe Nudell, Minnie Quartey, Zion Mengesha, Connor Toups, John R. Rickford, Dan Jurafsky, and Sharad Goel, ["Racial disparities in automated speech recognition,"](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1915768117) _Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences_, 2020.
