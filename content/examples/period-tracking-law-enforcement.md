## What Happened

After the Supreme Court overturned Roe v. Wade in June 2022, period tracking apps became a focus of concern for reproductive rights advocates. These apps collect sensitive data: menstrual cycle timing, symptoms, sexual activity, and pregnancy attempts or losses.

This data, collected in a personal health context, could potentially be accessed by law enforcement investigating abortion-related activity through several mechanisms: direct requests to app developers, data broker purchases of location data showing visits to abortion providers, or subpoenas.

Data brokers already sell location data to law enforcement without requiring a warrant. Several major period tracking apps had privacy policies that allowed data sharing with third parties under certain conditions, including legal requests.

The data had been collected voluntarily and in good faith for personal health purposes. Its potential use in criminal investigation was not something users could reasonably have anticipated when they created accounts.

**Source:** See reporting from the Electronic Frontier Foundation and The Markup on period app privacy and law enforcement data access, 2022. See also: Maggie Astor, ["Which Period-Tracking Apps Will Share Your Data With Law Enforcement?"](https://www.nytimes.com/2022/06/28/us/politics/period-apps-abortion-data.html) _The New York Times_, June 2022.
