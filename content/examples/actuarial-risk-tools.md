## What Happened

Beginning in the 1990s, a number of US jurisdictions adopted actuarial risk assessment instruments in criminal sentencing and parole decisions. These tools — with names like LSI-R, PSA, and later COMPAS — use factors including age, employment history, residential stability, prior arrests, and social associations to produce a risk score predicting the likelihood that someone will be rearrested.

The tools were explicitly designed to reduce reliance on individual judicial discretion, which had been documented as inconsistent and biased. The argument was that statistical instruments, applied uniformly, would be more objective and more fair than unstructured human judgment.

Critics raised several objections. Using group-level statistics to make decisions about individuals means someone is penalized for belonging to a category, not for their own conduct. Using prior arrest records in jurisdictions with documented racial disparities in policing means the prediction encodes those disparities. Using factors like residential stability and employment history means socioeconomic disadvantage functions as a risk factor.

The debate that begins in the 1990s intensifies dramatically after 2016, when ProPublica's investigation of the COMPAS tool documents racial disparities in false positive and false negative rates. The fairness mathematics at the center of that dispute are already latent in the design decisions made here.

**Source:** Bernard Harcourt, _Against Prediction: Profiling, Policing, and Punishing in an Actuarial Age_, University of Chicago Press, 2007.
