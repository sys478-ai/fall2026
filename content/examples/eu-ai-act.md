## What Happened

The European Union AI Act was agreed by the European Parliament in March 2024 and entered into force in August 2024, with different provisions phasing in over a two-year implementation period.

The Act classifies AI systems by risk level. Systems presenting unacceptable risk — including real-time biometric identification in public spaces, social scoring by public authorities, and AI that exploits psychological vulnerabilities — are prohibited. High-risk systems face conformity assessments, transparency requirements, and human oversight obligations. General-purpose AI models face lighter requirements, with heavier obligations for systems above certain capability thresholds.

The Act was the first comprehensive AI regulatory framework globally. The European Commission began drafting it in 2021; the final text was substantially revised following the rapid emergence of large language models in 2022–2023, adding specific requirements for foundation model providers.

The Act immediately became a reference point for regulatory debates outside Europe. Supporters argued it provided necessary protections in a market that wasn't self-regulating; critics argued it was too rigid to accommodate rapidly developing technology and would disadvantage European companies relative to US and Chinese competitors. Civil liberties organizations noted that the law enforcement and national security carve-outs were broad enough to potentially permit exactly the kinds of uses the Act's risk framework appeared to restrict.

**Source:** European Parliament, "EU AI Act: first regulation on artificial intelligence," 2024.
