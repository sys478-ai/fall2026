## What Happened

In 2021, the Biden administration extended ICE's death-reporting requirement to cover detainees who died within 30 days of being released from custody. The change followed the death of a man who had contracted COVID-19 at the Adelanto detention center and died three days after ICE released him — a death that fell outside the existing reporting boundary and triggered no investigation.

The 30-day rule made that category of death visible: countable, reviewable, and attributable to conditions in detention.

In June 2026, ICE eliminated that requirement, returning to reporting only deaths that occur while someone is physically in custody. The number of deaths did not change. What changed was the boundary of what enters the official record — and therefore what gets investigated, reported to Congress, and entered into the public data.

ICE framed the change as administrative common sense: the agency should not be responsible for people after they leave custody. But the measurement boundary is not a neutral description of responsibility. It is a decision about what becomes visible, to whom, and under what obligations.

**Source:** Douglas MacMillan, "ICE to stop reporting deaths of newly released detainees, internal memo says," _The Washington Post_, June 4, 2026.
