## What Happened

In 1966, MIT computer scientist Joseph Weizenbaum published a description of ELIZA, a conversational program he had written. ELIZA's most famous script, DOCTOR, simulated a Rogerian psychotherapist by applying simple pattern-matching rules: if the user said "I feel X," ELIZA would respond "Why do you feel X?" or "Tell me more about feeling X." The program had no model of the user, no memory between sessions, and no semantic understanding.

Weizenbaum was surprised and disturbed by users' responses. His secretary — who knew the system was a program — asked him to leave the room so she could speak with it in private. Users attributed genuine insight and care to ELIZA. Some researchers suggested ELIZA could be deployed as a therapeutic tool.

Weizenbaum spent the following years writing _Computer Power and Human Reason_ (1976), arguing that the willingness to anthropomorphize computers was not a feature to be exploited but a human vulnerability to be resisted. He was particularly alarmed by proposals to automate psychotherapy, arguing that some human functions require genuine human presence — that the appearance of empathy and actual empathy are not interchangeable.

ELIZA predates modern AI by decades, but it demonstrates something that persists: conversational interaction reliably triggers social projection, regardless of what is actually happening inside the system.

**Source:** Joseph Weizenbaum, "ELIZA — A Computer Program for the Study of Natural Language Communication Between Man and Machine," _Communications of the ACM_, 1966.
