## What Happened

Google Flu Trends attempted to estimate flu activity in real time by analyzing patterns in flu-related search queries. Because search data was available faster than traditional public health surveillance reports, the system appeared to offer a significant improvement over existing methods.

For several years it attracted attention as a model for using behavioral data to track public health. But the system overestimated flu prevalence significantly, particularly during the 2012–13 flu season.

Researchers found that search behavior is not the same as infection. People search for flu-related terms because they feel sick, but also because the news is covering flu, because autocomplete suggests related terms, because a family member is sick, or because general health anxiety rises. When Google changed how search results were displayed, search patterns shifted. The data was produced through a media environment, a search platform, and user behavior — not through a direct measurement of disease.

**Source:** David Lazer, Ryan Kennedy, Gary King, and Alessandro Vespignani, ["The Parable of Google Flu: Traps in Big Data Analysis,"](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1248506) _Science_, 2014.
