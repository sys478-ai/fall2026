## What Happened

In September 2021, Frances Haugen, a former product manager at Facebook, provided tens of thousands of pages of internal documents to the Wall Street Journal and to Congress. She subsequently testified before the Senate Commerce Committee.

The documents included internal research on the effects of Facebook's products on users' mental health — particularly among teenage girls — as well as research on how recommendation algorithms affected political polarization and the spread of misinformation. Haugen argued that Facebook's leadership prioritized growth and engagement over safety, and was aware of harms its platform caused but chose not to act.

The response from some researchers was more complicated. Several academics whose work was cited in the leaked documents publicly objected to how it was characterized. The causal inference problem in platform research is genuinely hard: demonstrating that recommendation algorithms cause polarization requires evidence that internal correlational research doesn't provide.

The case clearly illustrates, regardless of how the disputed evidence resolves, the question of what a system optimized for engagement is actually maximizing — and whether engagement as a proxy captures what the people designing and using the system actually care about.

**Source:** Jeff Horwitz and Deepa Seetharaman, "Facebook Knows Instagram Is Toxic for Teen Girls, Company Documents Show," _The Wall Street Journal_, September 14, 2021.
