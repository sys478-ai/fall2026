Frank Rosenblatt at Cornell builds the Perceptron, a learning machine that adjusts its own weights in response to training examples. The Navy funds it; the press announces it as a machine that will soon walk, talk, see, and be conscious of its own existence.

Marvin Minsky and Seymour Papert's 1969 book _Perceptrons_ demonstrates that single-layer networks cannot learn certain logical functions, triggering a collapse in enthusiasm. Funding dries up. The first AI winter begins.

The Perceptron episode establishes a pattern that recurs throughout AI history: a genuine capability, inflated claims, institutional investment, discovery of limitations, withdrawal of support.
