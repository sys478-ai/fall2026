# NSF PI Presentation Priority Plan

## Goal for the next 1.5 weeks

Prepare a clear, credible story for an NSF PI presentation that shows:

1. a concrete educational/research artifact,
2. a coherent course and study design around it,
3. a plausible fall implementation plan,
4. outward-facing materials that make the work legible.

The main risk is not lack of work. The main risk is that the work appears scattered across too many partially built materials.

## Core framing

Use this sentence as the organizing claim:

> This year, I built a course-and-research framework for teaching anticipatory AI governance through a technically concrete neuromorphic anomaly-detection case.

Then show that the work has four layers:

1. **Centerpiece learning sequence**
   An interactive SNN / anomaly-detection module students can actually use.
2. **Course architecture**
   A semester structure that situates the module inside a broader socio-technical AI course.
3. **Research design**
   An IRB-ready study of conceptual grounding for anticipatory governance.
4. **Public-facing synthesis**
   A brief or prospectus that explains the intervention and its contribution.

## Priority order

### Tier 1: Must be presentation-ready

#### 1. Interactive SNN / anomaly detection learning sequence

This is the centerpiece and should absorb the most time.

Target output:

- plain-language explainer
- interactive activity
- student worksheet
- reflection prompts
- ELSI discussion questions
- pre/post assessment items
- instructor notes

Why first:

- It is the most concrete artifact.
- It ties directly to BRAID.
- It supports the course, the IRB, the website, NSF reporting, and a possible paper.
- It gives you something demo-able instead of merely describable.

#### 2. Website triage for legibility

Do not try to perfect the whole site.

Target output:

- one clean landing path for the course
- one clean landing path for the BRAID case study
- one page or section that clearly names the 4 workstreams
- visible path to the centerpiece SNN/anomaly sequence

Why second:

- The site already contains substantial material, but it is distributed across syllabus, topics, assignments, modules, field guide, and case-study sections.
- Presentation audiences will read coherence as progress.

### Tier 2: Strongly needed

#### 3. SYS 478 course shell

Target output:

- revised 15-week schedule
- 6-8 major assignments
- final project prompt
- final project rubric
- readings/media by week
- 3 fully built modules

Boundary:

Do not build all 30 class meetings.

#### 4. IRB and study design packet

Target output:

- study purpose
- research questions
- recruitment language
- consent language
- pre-course survey
- reflection prompts
- SNN pre/post questions
- final reflection prompt
- optional interview/focus-group protocol
- data analysis plan

Boundary:

It needs to be coherent and aligned, not perfect.

### Tier 3: Useful but should not steal time

#### 5. Outward-facing synthesis brief

Target output:

- 3-5 page internal brief or prospectus

Use it to stabilize language for:

- NSF reporting
- PI presentation
- future abstract or paper
- policy or ELSI brief

This should mostly be assembled from decisions already made in the other three streams.

## Recommended time split

For the next 1.5 weeks:

- **40%** Interactive SNN / anomaly sequence
- **20%** Website triage and presentation screenshots
- **20%** Course shell
- **15%** IRB packet
- **5%** Outward-facing synthesis brief

## 10-day sprint plan

### Day 1

- Freeze the narrative for the PI presentation.
- Define the exact components of the centerpiece learning sequence.
- Decide what the website must show and what it can ignore for now.

### Day 2

- Draft or revise the plain-language explainer.
- Outline the interactive activity step by step.
- Identify what visuals or screenshots you need for the presentation.

### Day 3

- Build the student worksheet.
- Write reflection prompts and ELSI discussion questions.
- Draft pre/post assessment items.

### Day 4

- Write instructor notes.
- Test the learning sequence end to end as a 45-75 minute experience.
- Tighten pacing and remove anything ornamental.

### Day 5

- Triage the website around the centerpiece.
- Make sure visitors can find:
  - the course frame,
  - the BRAID case study,
  - the anomaly-detection learning sequence,
  - the larger rationale.

### Day 6

- Finalize the 15-week course schedule.
- Lock the 3 fully built modules.
- Draft the 6-8 major assignments list.

### Day 7

- Draft the final project prompt and rubric.
- Assemble weekly readings/media into a usable list.
- Write a short summary of the course arc for slides.

### Day 8

- Draft the IRB packet skeleton.
- Write recruitment, consent, pre-course survey, and SNN pre/post instruments.
- Add final reflection and optional interview protocol.

### Day 9

- Write the outward-facing 3-5 page synthesis brief.
- Pull language from the course shell and IRB packet rather than starting fresh.
- Build slide-ready bullets from the brief.

### Day 10

- Build the PI presentation.
- Use screenshots of the site and centerpiece activity.
- End with what is complete, what is piloting in fall, and what data you will collect.

## Website triage recommendations

The current site appears to have strong content but weak presentation-level consolidation.

Notable drift to clean up:

- the home page currently functions as the syllabus
- the top nav mixes syllabus, assignments, bibliography, planning, and external materials
- the BRAID case study exists, but the relationship between course, case study, and research intervention is not yet maximally visible
- there appears to be a nav link to `/resources` without a matching top-level page

### Minimum website fixes before the presentation

1. **Clarify the home page**
   Make the front page visibly answer:
   - What is this course/site?
   - What is the BRAID case study?
   - What is the main learning intervention?

2. **Create one obvious “start here” path**
   Suggested structure:
   - Course home
   - Weekly schedule
   - Labs / learning sequence
   - BRAID case study
   - Final project

3. **Feature the centerpiece**
   Put the SNN/anomaly-detection sequence in a prominent place with a short explanation of why it matters.

4. **Show the research connection**
   Add a short section explaining:
   - this is a teaching intervention,
   - it is also a study of conceptual grounding for anticipatory governance,
   - BRAID is the concrete case.

5. **Hide planning clutter from presentation audiences**
   Internal planning pages can remain in the repo, but should not dominate visible navigation.

## What to say in the PI presentation

Use a simple arc:

1. **Problem**
   Students are often asked to discuss AI ethics abstractly, without enough technical grounding to reason about emerging systems.

2. **Intervention**
   I designed a course and research framework that teaches anticipatory governance through hands-on engagement with neuromorphic anomaly detection.

3. **Concrete artifact**
   The centerpiece is an interactive SNN/anomaly-detection learning sequence with assessment, reflection, and instructor support.

4. **Course context**
   The sequence sits inside a broader SYS course on AI as a socio-technical system.

5. **Research plan**
   The fall course functions as a site for studying how conceptual grounding helps students connect technical mechanisms to governance questions.

6. **Deliverables**
   Course shell, intervention, IRB-ready study design, and outward-facing synthesis materials.

7. **Next step**
   Pilot in fall, collect student data, refine, and convert to reporting/paper outputs.

## Decision rule when overwhelmed

If a task does not improve one of these three things, defer it:

- the centerpiece artifact,
- the clarity of the presentation story,
- readiness to teach/study the work in fall.

## Short version

If time gets tight, protect these four outputs:

1. a polished SNN/anomaly learning sequence,
2. a cleaned-up website path that makes the work legible,
3. a usable course shell with 3 fully built modules,
4. a coherent IRB packet.

Everything else should serve those.
